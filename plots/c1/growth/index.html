Shapes gravity makes

Sixteen real numerical studies of the same law of gravity that end up nowhere near each other. Escape, an eternal shared loop, a full stop, a loop that only holds for a while, three separate circles that never stop agreeing, a wobble that holds forever around a corner with nothing sitting in it, a twin corner that was never going to hold at all, a whisper split two ways from the point between the two bodies themselves into a plunge and a wide unsettled wander, and, from the weakest, slowest unstable point of all, on the Sun's far side, the same whisper coming back around as a horseshoe instead of leaving at all — the same bounded wobble as the corner that holds, mirrored below the line instead of above it, sixty degrees behind Jupiter instead of sixty ahead — that same holding corner pushed past the mass ratio it actually needs, at a real pair of worlds instead of an invented one, spiraling loose and gone for good — the same corner swept across six mass ratios at once, the same nudge every time, watching the one threshold every other piece here only showed from one side at a time — the wall of unreachable space every one of those five equilibrium points turns out to be a pinch-point of, the same conserved number every earlier piece checked and never showed, swept until it breaks open one gate at a time — a shove instead of a whisper from the one corner that holds, in every direction and speed at once, finding that being kinematically free of that wall and staying caught in Jupiter's resonance are two different things, by a boundary that depends on which way you push, not just how hard — and, finally, chasing down the handful of directions that boundary never broke, out to eleven times the speed first tested, finding every one of them finite after all, some by the familiar close pass to Jupiter and, for the first time here, some by sailing straight past it unbothered — and, finally, rechecking every one of those seventeen critical speeds against three different solver tolerances, finding sixteen genuinely solid and one that depends on which clock you trust.

Three bodies, at rest, and no periodic future

Burrau's 1913 case: two bodies tangle into a tight binary, the third is slingshotted out for good. Chaotic — never repeats.

A curve three bodies agree to share forever

Moore's 1993 figure-eight: three bodies chase each other around one shared loop, forever. Stable — exactly repeats.

Three bodies that agree to become one point

Euler's 1767 collinear case: the same law, in a straight line instead of a triangle — collapses on itself in finite time, all three at once.

A loop that stops agreeing with itself

The figure-eight again, nudged by 3% at the start. Thirty-some laps look identical, then the shared curve quietly stops being shared.

Three bodies that never stop agreeing

Lagrange's 1772 triangle: any three masses, set at an equilateral triangle's corners with the right spin, rotate together forever, each on its own circle.

The point that only holds if nothing else is there

Lagrange's triangle again, but with the third mass shrunk to nothing — the real reason Jupiter's Trojan asteroids exist. Bounded forever, never exactly repeating.

The point that never holds

The restricted problem's other three equilibria, on the Sun-Jupiter line itself, are all unstable. A millionth of the separation, released at L2, takes eight years to show it — then never comes back.

Two ways to fall from the same point

L1, between the Sun and Jupiter, is unstable both ways. The same millionth-of-a-separation whisper, one direction: a tight spiral into Jupiter in 24 years. The other: a wide, unsettled sweep past the Sun.

The point that lets go gently

L3, behind the Sun, is unstable fifty times more slowly than L1 or L2. The same whisper doesn't escape at all — it comes back around as a horseshoe after one 767-year lap.

The corner sixty degrees behind

L5, Jupiter's trailing Trojan corner, is L4's exact mechanical mirror — same eigenvalues to the last digit. Same bounded loop-of-loops, reflected below the line, in the red astronomers actually use for it.

The corner that stops holding

Lagrange's triangle holds for any masses — but only if they're lopsided enough. At Pluto and Charon's real, far more even ratio, the same corner that holds for Jupiter's Trojans spirals loose and gets slingshotted away for good.

The corner that barely moves

The same corner, the same nudge, six mass ratios straddling Routh's threshold. The position slides by exactly Δμ, dead linear, no jump — only the dynamics bifurcate, from a bounded loop to a widening escape.

The wall that pinches at every point here

Every point on this plot marks where one conserved wall — the same Jacobi constant every piece here has quietly checked — pinches shut. Swept across six energy levels: two gates open in the same breath, a third opens alone, and the last two shrink to nothing.

What a shove from L4 finds that a whisper never could

Below L4's own Jacobi constant, gates.html found no wall left anywhere. 2,304 kicks from L4, every direction and speed at once, find a real boundary anyway — ragged, direction-dependent, laced with thin resonance-overlap slivers — between staying caught in Jupiter's orbit and breaking free of it.

No direction holds forever

resonance.html left a stubborn handful of directions untested past v=0.06. Pushed those out to eleven times that speed: all of them finally break, some the familiar way, a close pass to Jupiter, and some a new way — sailing straight past it, unbothered, toward the Sun instead.

The corner where three clocks disagree

escape-speed.html's own critical speeds, rechecked at three solver settings apiece. Sixteen of seventeen directions agree with themselves to a tenth of a percent or far better. One doesn't: the most resistant direction on the whole disc disagrees with itself by 27% — and this plot's usual Jacobi-constant check can't tell you which run to believe.
