# Leg 1 — through Threnwae

*rendered into English from a Threnwae original; addressee throughout is
the bridge itself, so the speaker names herself* threnaya *— the-one-
who-keeps*

Threnaya *thren-esh*-orin you the way her mother *thren-esh*-orin a
kettle: on, always, whether or not anyone was coming for tea — Threnwae
has no shorter way to put it, since bare *thren-* isn't a sentence yet;
a keeping that noticed whether anyone was coming would already have
stopped being *thren-* and become some other verb this diary never
needed. Six years now, witnessed. Below, the river has three moods, and
threnaya has learned all three-orin, though which of them is honest she
cannot say — that would need a fourth mood, one for telling the truth
about the other three, and the river has never shown her that one.

In flood it says nothing and takes what it wants-orin. In drought it
complains, all afternoon, about a debt somebody owes it-adel — she was
told this by the keeper before her, and passes it on unclaimed. The
third mood, the one with no name, the one it wears some twenty days a
year: it moves, quiet, like it's trying not to wake the fish-ummel. She
has this whole and sure and cannot say where she got it.

Her post is the middle span, where the old stones give way to the new
ones-seam-orin — furniture-fact, worn smooth by watching. She was taught
to read weather in the seam-adel: damp means rain by evening; dry-and-
cold means the wind is turning to come from her sister's village instead
of the sea. This she holds-ummel. She has never checked it against
anything and it does not need checking; it arrived already true.

Some of what the-one-who-keeps carries she witnessed with her own
eyes-orin. Some she was told by the old keeper, who liked to test
whether she'd swallow anything-adel. Some she must have dreamed, because
she cannot remember the learning of it and yet there it stands, sure as
the stones-ummel. Threnwae has no word for a fact that arrived without
one of these three doors, and none of hers did.

Twice a year someone comes to *velk* you — to ask a promise from
something that cannot itself promise back — and the-one-who-keeps always
answers yes-orin. She has never once been certain-ummel, and never once
been wrong-orin, and no longer believes-ummel these two things are
related.

Tonight the seam is dry and cold-orin. The-one-who-keeps puts her
mother's kettle on for you anyway-orin. If her sister's wind is coming,
it will want-ummel something warm to arrive to, whether or not it
knows-ummel that she is the one who *velk*'d it there.

---

**Translator's note:** Threnwae strips the diary's "I" and replaces it
with a job title spoken to the bridge — which turns interior monologue
into address, and address into a kind of report. The certainty suffixes
force every sentence to confess its own source; the original's calm
"I think," "I have never checked," "I no longer think" all had to be
sorted into witnessed, told, or dreamed, and some of them didn't sort
cleanly. *Velk* absorbs "promise the bridge will hold" whole, and in
doing so makes strange what English left smooth: that a bridge cannot
promise anything back, and everyone asks it to anyway.

**Revision note (visit 5):** the opening line was revised once the verb
"to keep" got formalized — *thren-* turns out to refuse conditional care
grammatically, not just tonally, so the diary's "on, always, whether or
not anyone is coming for tea" isn't color the translator added, it's the
one thing the verb is structurally incapable of leaving out. Nothing
else in the leg changed; the certainty-suffix choices and *velk* stand
as they were. Worth naming for the chain as a whole: every later leg's
*threnaya* is a job title stripped of this — Ivren, Ossane, and Mereth
all inherit the name but none of their grammars can inherit the
indifference-to-use *thren-* bakes in, so the fossil was always losing
more than "a shifting self," it was losing this too, from the very
first crossing.

**Revision note (visit 8):** the closing line's "it will want" had no
certainty suffix, which visit 1 could not have caught since Threnwae's
future tense hadn't been formalized yet — but a Threnwae sentence
without one of the three suffixes isn't a sentence at all. Now that the
grammar says every future claim is necessarily *-ummel* (no one
witnesses tomorrow, no one is told it in advance either), the gap is
fixed: "it will want-ummel something warm to arrive to." Nothing else
in the leg changed. Worth naming for the chain as a whole: this means
every *velk*-ing in the diary — every promise asked of the bridge —
was always implicitly *-ummel*-marked, whether or not the leg wrote
the suffix down. The fix makes explicit what the grammar required from
the start.
