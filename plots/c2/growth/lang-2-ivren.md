# Ivren

*a living sketch, extended over future visits*

Ivren is spoken by people with no grammatical way to say a thing simply
*is* someone's. There is no genitive, no possessive pronoun, no "'s," no
"of mine." Every relation between a person and a thing (or a person and
a place, or a person and another person) has to be named as an ongoing
verb — because in Ivren, belonging is not a fact, it's an activity, and
activities can stop.

**What it loses:** ownership as a state. Nothing simply belongs to
anyone in Ivren; it is always mid-relation, and the relation has to be
one of four kinds, chosen and marked every time:

- *tends* (*-vash*) — active, chosen care. You could stop and the thing
  would still exist, just untended. "The kettle my mother tends"
  (*aroth vash-eni*), not "my mother's kettle."
- *dwells-with* (*-oru*) — cohabitation, no hierarchy of ownership on
  either side. "The bridge we dwell-with" (*senket oru-mai*), not "our
  bridge." Neither party keeps the other; they're just both there.
- *owes* (*-ken*) — a debt relation, something that flows one direction
  and is understood to want returning, whether or not it ever will be.
- *binds-to* (*-thal*) — compulsory, can't be put down. Not chosen, not
  necessarily wanted. A post, a post, a debt inherited rather than
  incurred, a name you didn't pick. "The post I am bound-to"
  (*sethem thal-eni*), not "my post."

**What it imposes:** a forced diagnosis, every time you mention a
relation, of *what kind of hold* it is. English lets "my" cover
everything from a kettle you love to a job you resent to a war you
didn't start; Ivren refuses the shortcut. A speaker must decide, mid-
sentence, whether this is care, cohabitation, debt, or compulsion — and
sometimes the honest answer changes the meaning of the sentence more
than the speaker wants it to.

**Untranslatable words (so far):**

- *revka* — the specific, quiet exhaustion of still marking something
  *-vash* (tended) long after you've stopped being sure it needs your
  tending, and choosing the verb anyway because stopping would be its
  own kind of statement.
- *senket-oru* — used loosely for any large fixed structure (a bridge,
  a house, a wall) that a person dwells with rather than owns; carries
  a faint sense that the structure was there in some form before you
  and will be after, so cohabitation is the only honest verb.

**A borrowing, not yet explained:** Ivren has no relational self-naming
the way Threnwae does — no word that shifts with who you're addressing.
But *threnaya* appears in Ivren speech anyway, frozen, used as a fixed
epithet for anyone who tends a bridge for a living, the way a loanword
survives after the grammar that produced it is gone. Ivren speakers
who use it don't know it once meant "the-one-who-keeps" and shifted
with its addressee; to them it's just a job title, worn smooth.

**Left for later visits:** whether *-ken* (owed) can ever resolve — does
Ivren have a verb for a debt actually being paid off, or is *-ken*
permanent once marked? — and what happens grammatically when two people
disagree about which of the four verbs applies to the same thing.
