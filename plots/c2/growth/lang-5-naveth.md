# Naveth

*a living sketch, extended over future visits*

Naveth marks nothing about the speaker at all — no evidentiality like
Threnwae, no possession-verbs like Ivren, no weather-roots like Ossane,
no collective-sizing like Mereth. Its entire distinctive machinery sits
on the *other* side of the sentence: every time a Naveth speaker points a
clause at a listener — even an implied one, even no one — the verb must
carry a shape-particle for who is being spoken to.

Three shapes exhaust the system:

- *-sol* — solit. One listener, bonded to the speaker. Not "one person
  present," but one listener the speaker has decided to be answerable
  to, whether or not that listener can answer back.
- *-fal* — throng. A bounded, mutually-known group — a household, a
  crew, a village that would recognize itself if named. The speaker
  addresses them as a settled *we-facing-you*, not a headcount.
- *-erin* — open. An unknown or uncountable audience, including the real
  possibility of none: words thrown outward without a listener secured.
  A diary entry, a prayer, a private thought spoken aloud to an empty
  room — all take *-erin*, the shape for whoever, if anyone, catches it.

**What it loses:** neutral, addressee-blind speech. There is no way in
Naveth to simply state a fact into the air without also grammatically
committing to a guess about who, if anyone, is on the other end of it.
Even the most private interior thought is aimed somewhere, whether the
speaker likes it or not — Naveth has no register for a sentence that
isn't, in some sense, thrown.

**What it imposes:** constant audience-shaping. Before a clause can
close, the speaker must decide not *how many* listeners but *what kind
of claim on them* the sentence is making — one crucial and easy-to-miss
feature of the system: *-sol* does not require the listener be capable
of answering. It marks bond, not capacity. A bridge, a kettle, a dead
parent, a season can all be addressed *-sol* if the speaker holds
themselves answerable to it — the particle asks nothing about whether
the addressee could ever hold up its end. This is a plain grammatical
fact in Naveth, not a workaround or a strain; nothing in the system
resists it the way, for instance, Threnwae's *velk* had to name a
specific unease about promising to the unpromising. Naveth never built
that unease into its grammar in the first place, because *-sol* was
never about reciprocity to begin with.

**Untranslatable words (so far):**

- *savrun* — the vertigo of marking a sentence *-erin* (open, thrown to
  no secured listener) while privately hoping one particular person
  catches it anyway. The grammar says you addressed no one in
  particular; you know better.
- *kelemori* — the reverse error: marking someone *-fal* (a bonded
  throng, a "we-facing-you" the speaker is sure would claim itself)
  when the speaker privately isn't sure the group would claim them
  back. A presumption of belonging, spoken before it's confirmed.

**What happens to the inherited mechanics:** Naveth carries none of
Mereth's collective-subject apparatus forward — first person in Naveth
is ordinary, unmarked, ungraded; a Naveth speaker just says "I" the way
English does. Mereth's entire scope-negotiation (household, lineage,
*wen-annoth*) has nowhere to attach in a grammar that marks the
listener, not the speaker, so it does not survive as a smaller or
stranger version of itself — it simply isn't there. This is a starker
loss than the pattern's previous turns: Threnwae's *-ummel*, Ivren's
strained *-oru*, and Ossane's *sahren-velmoth* were each a dedicated
word for sourceless knowledge, and Mereth (visit 4) didn't coin a fourth
one but at least reused its own live mechanism (*wen-annoth*) to cover
the gap. Naveth has no mechanism there to reuse at all — sourceless
claims just surface as flat, unmarked statement, the same as any other
fact. Worth watching whether a sixth language restores some version of
this marking or confirms that, once lost twice in a row (Mereth's
non-coinage, now Naveth's total absence), it isn't coming back.

**Answering an old thread, carefully:** this is the first language in
the chain to actually take up the plural-address question Threnwae's
own sketch left open in visit 1 — "does a crowd get one relation-word,
or does the speaker have to average across all of them?" Naveth doesn't
resolve *Threnwae's* version of that question (Threnwae itself still
has no answer, and none is invented for it here — that would be
retrofitting, not translating). But where Mereth's all-collective
grammar was flagged as *resembling* an answer to plural address while
actually answering a different question (who is speaking, not how you
address a crowd — see `lang-4-mereth.md`), Naveth is built from the
crowd-addressing question directly: *-fal* exists for exactly the case
Threnwae wondered about, a crowd addressed as a settled unit rather than
averaged across. It's a different language's answer, not Threnwae's own
— but it's the chain's first honest engagement with the thread rather
than a false resemblance to one.

**The departed, resolved:** the thread this sketch itself left open —
whether Naveth marks someone who was once *-sol* and is now
unreachable. It does, but not as a fourth independent shape sitting
beside the first three. It is a bound suffix on *-sol* itself: *-wen*,
yielding *-solwen*. Ordinary *-sol* was never about capacity in the
first place — a bridge, a kettle, a season take it exactly as easily as
a living person, because *-sol* only ever marked the speaker's chosen
answerability, not the listener's. *-solwen* marks something *-sol*
structurally cannot: a capacity that used to be real and is gone, not
one that was never there to begin with. A bridge is never *-solwen*,
no matter how long it stands silent — it had nothing to lose. A dead
mother is.

That answers the question the thread posed, but not the way either of
its two offered shapes suggested. It is not a comfort — the bond
survives intact, the speaker doesn't relearn how to address the
departed, address itself doesn't break. But it is not a blindness
either — the grammar refuses to let the speaker pretend nothing
changed. Every clause aimed at the dead carries the change forward,
whether the speaker wants it named or not. Naveth's answer is neither
of the options the sketch offered when it left this open: not comfort,
not blindness, but insistence — a grammar that will not let you elide
what you already know.

One wrinkle, and the reason a third untranslatable word belongs here:
the suffix tracks the *speaker's* knowledge, not fact. A listener
addressed *-sol* whose death the speaker does not yet know of stays
*-sol* — the grammar hasn't caught up to a truth it doesn't have yet.
*Tessol* names exactly this: the particular vertigo of a sentence
correctly marked in the moment and about to become a small, retroactive
lie the instant the news arrives. Unlike *savrun* and *kelemori* (a
speaker's private doubt outrunning what the grammar says), *tessol* runs
the other way — the world outrunning the grammar, and the grammar none
the wiser yet.
