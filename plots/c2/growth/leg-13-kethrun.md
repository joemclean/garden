# Leg 13 — through Kethrun

*rendered into English from a Kethrun original, itself translated from
the Sennavor of leg 12, not from Corveth, Threshel, Calvorn, Vaelith,
Torvane, Vendrel, Naveth, Mereth, Ossane, Ivren, Threnwae, or the source
before them — the chain still moves one link at a time. Kethrun mints no
new names of its own; every referent here is the compound leg 8 settled
and every leg since has carried forward whole — vessa-nirel, vessa-esk,
sohven-esk, kesta-esk, yenna-esk, aksa-esk, aesa-nirel, kelvet. What
Kethrun adds is a mark on every finite verb declaring whose hand the act
is in: *-aveth* (opened — no namable hand before it), *-yorel* (kept —
received from a hand before the speaker's), or *-sennoth* (left —
aimed at a hand not yet arrived).*

Vessa-nirel keeps-*yorel* the kettle lit — received whole from her
mother, who kept-*aveth* it before her. Not the same mark for the same
act across the two women, the way Sennavor found the same mark for
both: the leg has no hand to name before the mother's, so Kethrun's
default falls where the record runs out, not where the practice
actually began. *Avethkir* sits exactly here, unstated but live under
the mark — the mother's keeping may have had a hand behind it too, one
this leg simply has no way to reach.

Below, sohven-esk wears three moods, and none of them takes *-yorel*.
A river does not receive a practice from a hand before it the way a
taught skill does; whatever it does each time, it does without a
teacher, and Kethrun has no honest way to mark it otherwise. In flood
it takes-*aveth* what it wants, announcing nothing. In drought it
complains-*aveth*, all afternoon, of a debt owed it. The third mood,
unnamed across twelve languages before this one, moves-*aveth* too,
"like it's trying not to wake the fish" — three moods, three instances
of the identical mark, for the identical reason: nothing upstream of the
river taught the river to be the river.

My post is the middle span, and here the leg finds its clearest case
of the mark built for exactly this: yenna-esk taught-*sennoth*
vessa-nirel to read the seam. A lesson is not a repetition of something
received — it is the act of making something to be received, staked on
a listener who has to still be there to take it. *Sennavesh* is the
bet inside that single verb: yenna-esk could not have known, teaching
it, that vessa-nirel would keep the practice six years running rather
than let it lapse the first winter he was gone. Everything since is
vessa-nirel reading the seam on her own, and every one of those
readings happens-*yorel* — hers now, but never opened by her; six
years of a hand doing what another hand's teaching set going, and
*yorenthe* is the specific weight of not being able to say anymore
whether she keeps reading it because she chooses to or only because no
one has ever asked her to stop.

Her certainty and her rightness split under this axis in a way none of
the twelve before it split them. The promise itself — being
asked-*yorel*, answering-*yorel* — is not new work; a keeper is
asked because keepers have always been asked, the post itself received
along with everything it obliges, whether or not this specific asking
was ever separately taught. But "I have never once been certain" and "I
no longer think these two facts are related" open-*aveth*, both of
them, private reckonings with no hand behind them at all — nobody
taught her to doubt her own yes, and nobody taught her to notice the
doubt and the rightness weren't touching. The outward shape of the duty
is received; what she privately makes of carrying it is entirely,
freshly her own, opened new each time she thinks it. Her rightness — six
years unbroken, visible to anyone who crosses — is stranger still: it
keeps-*yorel*, the same mark as the promise, because a bridge that has
held every day since a hand before her raised it is not opening
anything, it is simply still standing on what was already built.

Tonight the seam runs dry and cold. Vessa-nirel lights-*yorel* the
kettle anyway, the same mark, the same hand, as the leg's opening line.
But the line that follows breaks the pattern the opening set: if
aesa-nirel is coming, it will want-*sennoth* something warm to arrive
to, "whether or not it knows I am the one who put it there" — not a
continuation of anything received, but an act staked forward, on a
hand that has not yet arrived and may never confirm it did. *Sennavesh*
again, the second and final time this leg needs it: the diary opens on
a kept hand reaching back to a mother and closes on a left hand
reaching forward to no one it can name.

**Translator's note:** Kethrun's mandatory mark costs this leg an
inventory none of the twelve before it took directly — not of
knowledge, hold, weather, collective, listener, negation, cause, name,
count, edge, condition, or address, but of lineage: whose hand an act
is in, coming or going. Every predecessor asked something about the act
itself or its audience; Kethrun asks something about its place in a
chain of hands, and the source, once read this way, turns out to be
built almost entirely out of exactly that chain — a kept kettle, a
taught seam, a received post, a left warmth.

**On the bookend, broken rather than confirmed:** three languages
running — Threnwae, Naveth, Sennavor — each found the diary's opening
and closing lines doing the *same* grammatical thing, three different
ways of saying "indifferent to whether anyone's there." Kethrun is the
first to look at the same two lines and find them doing opposite work:
the opening kettle-line reaches backward, *-yorel*, a practice received
from a hand already given; the closing kettle-line reaches forward,
*-sennoth*, a practice staked on a hand not yet received. Checked
before trusting this as a real break rather than a coincidence of
which mark got assigned: the act is literally the same act, the kettle
lit either way — what differs is only which direction the hand in it
points, backward to a mother or forward to a wind. Three languages
found the diary saying the same thing twice. Kethrun finds it saying
one thing and its mirror.

**On the river:** *-aveth* three times running, for a different reason
than the certainty paragraph's *-aveth* pair — the river's mark is
forced by what a river structurally is (nothing taught it), the private
reckoning's is Kethrun's default for evidence that's simply run out.
Worth keeping the two kinds of *-aveth* distinct rather than reading
them as one finding: this leg's grammar can't tell them apart on the
surface, only the translator's note can.

**On the promise versus the doubt beneath it:** the leg splits the
certainty/rightness pair along a line no predecessor drew — outward
duty received, inward reckoning opened — rather than along
voiced/withheld (Sennavor), whole/mending (Corveth), or any prior
language's own cut. Not a better split than those, a different question
put to the same two sentences.

**Fossil, unchanged:** *threnaya* does not appear in this leg, for an
eighth leg running, for the same reason as the sixth through twelfth:
leg 12's own text never carries the word forward, so leg 13 has nothing
upstream to find either.

**What happens to the inherited mechanics:** see `lang-13-kethrun.md`'s
own accounting — Torvane's causation-marking and Vaelith's naming-rule
stay exactly as dormant here as they already were in Calvorn, Threshel,
Corveth, and Sennavor. No other language's surface mark is live in this
leg's grammar at all — the standing one-grammar-per-leg rule since
Ossane — so there was nothing this crossing to check for competition,
unlike Threshel, Corveth, and Sennavor, which each had a genuinely live
neighbor to check against.

**Left for later:** whether *-sennoth* is ever chosen where an *-aveth*
or *-yorel* reading would also have been honestly available is
untested — this leg's one *-sennoth* instance (the closing line) had no
real competing reading; want-*sennoth* was simply what the line was.
The standing chain-wide menu is otherwise unchanged: Ossane's
weather-pun tension, open since visit 3 by design and not waiting on
anything.
