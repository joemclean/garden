# Leg 12 — through Sennavor

*rendered into English from a Sennavor original, itself translated from
the Corveth of leg 11, not from Threshel, Calvorn, Vaelith, Torvane,
Vendrel, Naveth, Mereth, Ossane, Ivren, Threnwae, or the source before
them — the chain still moves one link at a time. Sennavor mints no new
names of its own; every referent here is the compound leg 8 settled and
every leg since has carried forward whole — vessa-nirel, vessa-esk,
sohven-esk, kesta-esk, yenna-esk, aksa-esk, aesa-nirel, kelvet. What
Sennavor adds is a mark on every finite verb declaring who the act is
for: *-thurel* (voiced — reaching for a witness), *-kessin* (withheld —
refusing one on purpose), or *-morrin* (incidental — indifferent to
whether one exists at all).*

Vessa-nirel keeps-*morrin* the kettle lit. Not withheld — she hides
nothing about doing it — and not voiced either, since no one is being
told or shown; the keeping simply runs on terms that have nothing to do
with an audience, "whether or not anyone is coming for tea." Her mother
kept-*morrin* it before her, the identical mark for the identical
reason across both women: the source's own opening line settles this
question before Sennavor even has to ask it.

Below, sohven-esk wears three moods, and this leg finds a different
orientation in each. In flood it comes-*kessin* — over the bank, taking
what it wants, announcing none of it; the taking is total, but "it says
nothing" is not an absence of expression, it's a refusal of one, the
starkest *-kessin* the leg has to offer, a withholding so complete it
doesn't even flinch at its own scale. In drought it stays low and
complains-*thurel*, all afternoon, of a debt aksa-esk owes it — the
loudest verb in the leg, insistent on being heard whether or not
anyone's listening, wanting an audience even where none is offered one.
The third mood, unnamed across eleven languages before this one,
moves-*morrin* — not raging like the first, not performing its
complaint like the second, just moving carefully, "like it's trying not
to wake the fish": an act genuinely indifferent to being noticed,
which is not the same as an act trying to avoid it.

My post is the middle span, and yenna-esk taught-*thurel* vessa-nirel
to read the seam there — a lesson is, by its own nature, oriented
toward a witness; teaching that reached no one wouldn't be teaching.
Everything since is vessa-nirel reading the seam on her own, and every
one of those readings happens-*morrin* — not concealed from anyone (she
has never lied about doing it), not performed for anyone either (she
has never once told the result to another soul, this leg included,
until now); the readings simply occur, six years running, on terms that
never asked whether they'd be known.

Her certainty and her rightness are, as they have been for language
after language before this one, the hardest single case in the source,
and Sennavor's own axis answers it differently than any predecessor's.
Her yes to the asking is voiced-*thurel* — a promise is
definitionally an act meant to reach another ear. Beneath it, unmarked
by the same breath that speaks the yes, "I have never once been
certain" sits withheld-*kessin*: a doubt
she has kept, on purpose, from ever surfacing through six years of
saying yes anyway — *renneth*, the exact weight of that pairing, a
voiced promise and a withheld doubt occupying one breath without either
canceling the other. Her rightness — six years unbroken, visible to
anyone who has ever crossed — is not voiced either; she has never once
declared it. It happens-*morrin*, evident to whoever happens to be
there without ever being performed for them, the same register as the
kettle and the seam-readings. And "I no longer think these two facts
are related" is *-morrin* too: a private reckoning, written here,
oriented toward no reader in particular, the diary's most
confession-shaped sentence turning out, once marked, not to be a
confession at all.

Twice a year aksa-esk comes asking-*thurel* a promise of kesta-esk, and
she answers-*thurel* — a request and its yes are both oriented at each
other by definition, the one place in the leg where two consecutive
verbs share the same mark for the same reason. But the promise's own
keeping — the six months of nightly rounds that make the yes true —
reverts to *-morrin* the moment the asking is over, the identical
register the kettle has always worn. The source's own simile, "I keep
the bridge the way my mother kept her kettle," turns out in Sennavor to
be exact rather than poetic: both are *-morrin* acts, the promise's
performed *-thurel* moment spent, its long fulfillment indifferent to
witness ever after.

Tonight the seam runs dry and cold. Vessa-nirel lights-*morrin* the
kettle anyway, the same mark as the leg's first line. Whether aesa-nirel
is coming stays *-morrin* too — a want that will "arrive to" whatever's
there for it, "whether or not it knows I am the one who put it there" —
*tarnesh* exactly, an act aimed at no one arriving to someone anyway,
warmth received by a wind that was never its intended witness. The
source's own closing construction hands this leg its second and
final bookend: the diary opens and closes on the identical mark, the
same incidental care, stated twice.

**Translator's note:** Sennavor's mandatory mark costs this leg a
running inventory none of the eleven before it took directly — not of
knowledge, hold, weather, collective, listener, negation, cause, name,
count, edge, or condition, but of address: who, if anyone, an act is
oriented toward. Where every predecessor asked something about the act
itself, Sennavor asks something about its intended audience, and the
source hands it the clearest possible material: its own opening and
closing lines both already say, in English, "whether or not" — the
exact shape of an act declared indifferent to witness before any
invented grammar ever touched it.

**On the bookend:** no prior language found the opening and closing
lines to be doing the same grammatical thing. Threnwae's *thren-*
(visit 5) explained the opening line alone, from the verb's own
refusal of conditional care; Naveth's *thurnai* (visit 6) landed the
fossil-name on the same image from a listener-shaped grammar, but
addressed only the opening. Sennavor is the first axis built around
exactly the dimension both lines share — orientation to witness — and
finds them identical: both are *-morrin*, stated once at the diary's
first line and once at its last, the clearest single-word summary this
whole chain has produced yet of what kind of keeping this diary is
actually about.

**On the sourceless-knowledge progression:** this leg does not extend
it, and for a reason worth naming rather than a lapse. A run of prior
devices has already answered how a fact without a traceable origin gets
marked — a suffix, a strained borrowing, a compound, a reused mechanic,
an absence, a forced revival, and Corveth's own collapse-naming word.
Every one of them asks the same underlying question: *how was this
known?* Sennavor doesn't ask that question at all — its axis is who an
act is for, not how it arrived — so "I think this is true. I have never
checked" and its restored seam-reading twin get no new device. What they get
instead is a mark on a different fact entirely: both admissions are
*-morrin*, private reckonings never aimed at another ear, which
reframes the source's most confession-sounding lines as structurally
indifferent to being read rather than daringly exposed. Not a
continuation of the progression — a different question landing,
coincidentally, on the same two sentences.

**On the future-tense problem:** four languages before this one found
no way to mark *aesa-nirel*'s arrival as a finite verb — Threnwae for
want of a witnessed or told door, Calvorn for want of any tense
mechanic at all, Threshel for want of an edge already crossed into
being to measure, Corveth for want of a condition yet to assess.
Sennavor is the first to mark it cleanly: *-morrin* asks only whether
an act orients toward a witness, and that question doesn't require the
act to already exist any more than it requires a source, a count, or a
side of an edge — a want can be exactly as indifferent to being
witnessed before it happens as after. Worth naming as a break in the
pattern rather than a fifth instance of it: not every axis in this
chain was ever going to stumble on the not-yet the same way, the same
kind of checked-not-assumed result Torvane's inherited mechanic gave
this leg's own "what happens to inherited mechanics" section.

**Fossil, unchanged:** *threnaya* does not appear in this leg, for a
seventh leg running, for the same reason as the sixth through eleventh:
leg 11's own text never carries the word forward, so leg 12 has nothing
upstream to find either.

**What happens to the inherited mechanics:** see `lang-12-sennavor.md`'s
own accounting — Torvane's causation-marking and Vaelith's naming-rule
stay exactly as dormant here as they already were in Calvorn, Threshel,
and Corveth. Corveth's own repair-marking does not carry forward as a
live mechanic in this leg's grammar (no language's mark survives into
the next leg's surface text; only the underlying device sometimes gets
reused, the way Vaelith reused Torvane's causation for naming) — checked
against this leg directly rather than assumed dormant: a *-thurel* act
here is *-varne*, *-skorel*, and *-thendra* by turns with no visible
pattern (the teaching and the promise are both *-thurel* despite
resolving to different repair-marks under Corveth's own axis one leg
back), the plainest non-competing result since Torvane and Threshel
first found causation and threshold didn't crowd each other either.

**Left for later:** whether a *-thurel* act in this chain ever turns out
to have been *-morrin* all along — performed for a witness who was
never there to receive it — is untested; no passage in the source stages
that reversal directly, and forcing one in would mean inventing content
the chain's own rules don't allow. The standing chain-wide menu is
otherwise unchanged: Ossane's weather-pun tension, open since visit 3 by
design and not waiting on anything.
