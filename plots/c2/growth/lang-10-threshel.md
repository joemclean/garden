# Threshel

*a living sketch, extended over future visits*

Every finite verb in Threshel carries a mandatory threshold-mark, one of
three: *-imre* (nearside — the act is wholly contained on the speaker's
own side of whatever boundary is in play), *-oswin* (farside — wholly on
the boundary's other side), or *-kelvra* (astride — the act happens *at*
the threshold itself, belonging to neither side, or the act's whole
content *is* the crossing). There is no fourth option and no way to leave
a verb unmarked — the same absolute-coverage shape as Threnwae's
certainty suffixes, Torvane's causation marks, and Calvorn's iteration
marks, now applied to a fourth question entirely: not how you know, not
who caused it, not how many times, but *where*, relative to an edge.

Threshel does not fix in advance what the boundary is. A speaker
nominates it fresh for each clause — the seam in the bridge's stones, the
kettle-room's threshold, the riverbank, the line between what's known and
what isn't, even the line between one day and the next — the same live
choice Ossane's speakers make picking a weather-root, not a rule handed
down from outside the sentence. What Threshel *does* fix is that some
boundary must be nominated, every time, for every finite verb, even for
acts that in English need no edge at all to be true.

**What it loses:** any verb that simply happens, unlocated, the way
English "I keep the bridge" or "I think this is true" float free of any
edge. A Threshel speaker must also declare, every time, which side of
which line the act sits on — or that it sits on no side at all, which is
its own declaration, not an escape from making one.

**What it imposes:** a grammar that cannot describe a life without
constantly surveying its edges. To say an act happened at all is to also
locate it against some threshold, named or implied — the seam-reading
the source itself already made a skill ("I was taught to read the weather
in the seam") generalizes here into the one thing every sentence must do.

**Untranslatable words (so far):**

- *velmoth-tir* — the specific vertigo of noticing a threshold you had
  long marked fixed — a seam, a bank, a day's turning — has been sliding
  the whole time you called it still, so that every past *-imre* or
  *-oswin* built on it may have been mismeasured from a wrong edge, not a
  wrong side.
- *kelvrasse* — the ache particular to something that is *always*
  *-kelvra*, permanently astride, never once granted a clean side to
  rest on — the cost of being the threshold's own definition rather than
  merely standing near one.
- *oswintheth* — the discomfort of marking *-oswin* something that used
  to sit *-kelvra* in every prior telling of it — not because it moved,
  but because the speaker's own vantage did, and the mark can't say
  which.

**Roots used so far:** *-imre*, *-oswin*, *-kelvra* are Threshel's own
coinage, new morphology built for this visit. No root is borrowed from
any earlier language's vocabulary.

**What happens to the inherited mechanics:** checked, rather than
assumed either way, whether threshold-marking crowds out Torvane's
causation-marking (dormant since Calvorn, though its *output* — the
frozen epithets — still carries every clause a proper name) or Vaelith's
naming-rule (also non-productive since Calvorn, for the same reason: leg
9 minted no new referents to name, only reused leg 8's). Structurally,
neither would conflict even if both were still live: *-esk/-oth/-nirel*
asks who caused an act, the naming-rule asks how a referent may be
pointed at, and *-imre/-oswin/-kelvra* asks only where, relative to an
edge — three genuinely different questions, the same non-competing shape
that has held since Torvane first found it with Vendrel. But, as with
Calvorn, absence of conflict isn't a reason to revive either mechanic:
leg 10 translates leg 9's already-finished text, which already carries
every referent as a settled compound name (*vessa-nirel*, *vessa-esk*,
*sohven-esk*, *kesta-esk*, *yenna-esk*, *aksa-esk*, *aesa-nirel*,
*kelvet*) — nothing new to name, nothing new to cause. Both stay exactly
as dormant in Threshel as they already were in Calvorn.

**What happens to Calvorn's own mechanic:** checked honestly, and this
one is a real loss, not a dormancy. Calvorn's iteration marks
(*-tovid/-serai/-runda/-heth*) ask how many times an act has occurred
across a speaker's whole history — a question about a span of time, not
a position in space. Threshel's threshold-marking has no natural place
to put that information: an act that has happened once versus a thousand
times can sit *-imre*, *-oswin*, or *-kelvra* with equal ease: frequency
and edge-position simply don't interact, unlike Vendrel's
*-arden*/*sohu* and Torvane's causation marks, which shared no
competition *and* Torvane found active use for the older mechanic
underneath its own. Here there is nothing underneath to keep running —
Calvorn's mark answers a question Threshel's grammar has no slot for at
all, so it lapses on this crossing the same way Mereth's collective
apparatus lapsed in Naveth and Naveth's own listener-shapes lapsed in
Vendrel: a genuine total loss, the fourth one in the chain, not a
dormancy like the naming-rule and causation-marking above. Named plainly
rather than either forced or denied: some crossings lose a mechanic
because the new axis has no room for it, and this is one, for a clear,
checked reason rather than a guess.

**The deliberate crossing, resolved (visit 25):** whether *-kelvra* is
ever chosen over an available *-imre* or *-oswin*, rather than reached
for only where no clean side exists, is settled inside leg 10 itself —
see its visit 25 revision note. The teaching line shares its
crossing-shape with the promise passage a few lines later (something
moving from the speaker to another person), and the promise resolves
*-imre*, whole the moment it's spoken. Teaching could have followed the
same reading — the revision has the leg weigh it and refuse it on
purpose: a promise asks nothing of its receiver to be complete, a lesson
does, so marking it *-kelvra* names a different act than *-imre* would
have, not a fallback reached for because nothing else fit. Threshel now
holds both shapes of its own mark — astride forced, and astride
chosen — inside one leg, the same milestone Vendrel's *sohu* reached in
visit 13.

**The edge-pattern, resolved (visit 26):** whether a pattern exists in
which edges recur across leg 10, and how each recurrence gets marked, is
settled by actually walking the leg's distinct boundaries rather than
its individual clauses. Six boundaries get nominated, not ten or
twenty — several verbs share one:

- *Whether tonight belongs to someone coming or to no one* (the
  kettle-keeping): invoked three times (vessa-nirel's keeping, twice;
  her mother's, once) and marked *-kelvra* every single time. Never
  *-imre*, never *-oswin*.
- *The literal seam*, read as a threshold in its own right rather than
  stood on either side of it: invoked once directly ("reading it is, as
  always, *-kelvra*") but structurally present every time vessa-nirel
  reads it, and always *-kelvra*.
- *The riverbank*: invoked three times, once per mood, and takes all
  three marks — *-oswin* in flood, *-imre* in drought, *-kelvra* for the
  unnamed third mood.
- *The witnessed/unwitnessed line*: the busiest edge in the leg,
  invoked at least six times across vessa-nirel's own six years
  (*-imre*), her mother's told keeping (*-oswin*), every seam-reading
  she's performed since (*-imre*), her certainty (*-imre*), the
  bridge's own rightness (*-oswin*), and the one retrospective claim
  that the two used to meet (*-kelvra*, now explicitly declined —
  "Threshel has no word yet for noticing two things were never really
  touching, only nearby").
- *The teacher-student knowledge crossing*: invoked once, *-kelvra*,
  chosen rather than forced (see the deliberate-crossing entry above).
- *Promise-giving*: invoked twice (the promise paragraph, and the
  passage it's read against for the teaching revision), *-imre* both
  times.

The pattern is not which edges recur most, it's which edges ever
resolve off the center mark. Two boundaries — the kettle's
coming-or-no-one line and the seam taken as a threshold in itself —
are invoked more than once and never once take a side; not because no
speaker has tried, but because, as the leg's own third-river-mood
passage already said of a different edge, their whole character *is*
to occupy the line and refuse to resolve onto either side of it. A
covenant that has to hold for whoever might come cannot collapse to
"for someone" or "for no one" without stopping being that covenant; a
seam cannot be read from a side, only stood on. The other two recurring
boundaries — the riverbank and the witnessed/unwitnessed line — take
whatever mark the specific case earns, including, in the
witnessed/unwitnessed line's single *-kelvra*, a claim that a mark can
later retract once the two sides turn out never to have met. These are
ordinary edges, genuinely two-sided, and Threshel's grammar treats them
that way. So the pattern is real, but it isn't about frequency — it's
that a recurring edge is either constitutively astride (marked
*-kelvra* every time, by what the act *is*) or genuinely resolvable
(marked whatever a given instance actually earns). No leg content
changed; this is a survey of what leg 10 already does, not a revision
of it.
