# Sennavor

*a living sketch, extended over future visits*

Every finite verb in Sennavor carries a mandatory address-mark, one of
three: *-thurel* (voiced — done in a way that reaches, or tries to
reach, another's knowing: performed, declared, said to be heard),
*-kessin* (withheld — done in a way that actively refuses to reach
another: a choice, not an absence, to keep something from arriving),
or *-morrin* (incidental — done on terms that have nothing to do with
being witnessed at all: neither reaching for an audience nor hiding
from one). There is no fourth option and no way to leave a verb
unmarked — the same absolute-coverage shape Threnwae, Ossane, Mereth,
Naveth, Torvane, Calvorn, Threshel, and Corveth each built for their own
single question, now applied to a question none of them asked: not how
you know, not what kind of hold, not who caused it, not how many times,
not where relative to an edge, not what state of repair, but *who an
act is for*.

Sennavor does not restrict the mark to speech. A silence can be
*-thurel* if it is performed to be noticed (a held breath meant to be
heard as a held breath); an ordinary spoken sentence can be *-kessin*
if what it says is chosen precisely to keep something else from being
said; and the loudest visible act in the source — a river in flood —
turns out, on inspection, to need all three marks depending on exactly
which part of it a speaker means. The mark asks nothing about volume in
the literal sense and everything about orientation: does this act point
toward a witness, away from one, or nowhere in particular.

**What it loses:** any verb that simply happens, unremarked on whether
it was meant for anyone. Corveth's condition-marking, Threshel's
edge-marking, Torvane's causation — none of them ask this question, and
none of their answers predict Sennavor's. A *-varne* act (whole,
sound) can be *-thurel*, *-kessin*, or *-morrin* with equal ease; state
of repair and orientation toward witness are independent facts about
the same clause, the way Corveth already found repair and edge mostly
were.

**What it imposes:** a confession, on every clause, of who the act was
for — including the speaker's own private thoughts, which English
allows to simply exist unaddressed. Sennavor cannot let a realization
occur without also declaring whether the speaker meant anyone to know
it, meant to keep it from anyone, or gave the question no thought
either way. There is no neutral middle a speaker can retreat to by
saying nothing about orientation — silence itself needs a mark, same as
speech.

**Untranslatable words (so far):**

- *renneth* — the specific weight of a *-thurel* act carrying, in the
  same breath, a *-kessin* fact directly beneath it: a yes said aloud
  with its doubt held back on purpose, promise and privacy occupying
  one utterance without either canceling the other.
- *tarnesh* — the vertigo of a *-morrin* act — one that never asked to
  be seen, never performed for anyone — being witnessed anyway and
  mistaken, from outside, for devotion: care that arrives to someone
  without ever having been aimed at them.

**Referents:** Sennavor mints no new names of its own. Every referent
here is the compound Vaelith settled and every language since has
carried forward whole — *vessa-nirel*, *vessa-esk*, *sohven-esk*,
*kesta-esk*, *yenna-esk*, *aksa-esk*, *aesa-nirel*, *kelvet*. Nothing
new to name, so nothing asked of the naming-rule this crossing either.

**What happens to the inherited mechanics:** checked, not assumed.
Torvane's causation-marking and Vaelith's naming-rule stay exactly as
dormant here as they were in Calvorn, Threshel, and Corveth — every
referent arrives already named, nothing new caused. Corveth's own
repair-marking does not carry forward as a live mechanic either (each
language's leg speaks only in its own single grammar, the standing rule
since Ossane), but the two axes were checked side by side for the kind
of local coincidence Threshel and Corveth found at the seam: they
don't coincide anywhere in this leg. A *-thurel* act can be *-varne*,
*-skorel*, or *-thendra* with no pattern favoring any pairing — the
teaching (landed, complete) is as *-thurel* as the promise (extended,
ongoing) despite one being *-imre*-shaped and the other *-thendra*-
shaped under Corveth's own axis. Orientation toward witness and state
of repair are simply independent, the plainest non-competing result
this chain has found since Torvane first noticed causation and
threshold didn't crowd each other either.

**The bookend, found in translation:** the source's opening and closing
lines both hinge on the identical construction — "whether or not
anyone is coming for tea," "whether or not it knows I am the one who
put it there" — and Sennavor is the first language built to notice that
these are not two separate stylistic tics but one grammatical fact
stated twice: both acts are, definitionally, *-morrin*. The keeper's
whole trade, on the evidence of her own two framing sentences, is
incidental work — care extended on terms that were never about being
seen doing it. No prior language had a mark for this specifically,
though several came close from other directions (Threnwae's *thren-*
found the same indifference-to-use inside the verb "to keep";
Sennavor finds it again from the opposite side, in whether the act
reaches for a witness at all).

**Left for later visits:** whether a *-thurel* act ever turns out, on
reflection, to have been *-morrin* all along — the speaker believing
she was performing for someone who was never there to receive it — is
untested; no leg occasion for it has been checked yet. Naveth's own
reverse-vertigo question (*avrasol*, resolved visit 27) is a mirror
shape to this one but lives inside a different axis (whether a listener
is still there) and does not answer it. Threnwae's still-unclaimed
plural-address question is not Sennavor's to answer, and this visit
didn't try.
