# Leg 4 — through Mereth

*rendered into English from a Mereth original, itself translated from the
Ossane of leg 3, not from Ivren, Threnwae, or the source before them —
the chain still moves one link at a time. Mereth has no weather-verbs,
no duration/will particles, and no individual first person; every verb
below has been re-anchored to whichever collective could plausibly also
be saying it.*

We-who-keep-this-post keep you the way we-of-the-hearth kept a kettle:
lit, always, whether or not anyone was coming for tea. Six years now,
we who stand in this post's line. Below, the river holds three moods,
and we have come to know all three, though which is honest we cannot
say — that would need a fourth mood, one for telling the truth about
the other three, and the river has never handed that mood to any of us
who've stood here.

In flood it says nothing and takes what it wants. In drought it
complains, all afternoon, about a debt someone owes it — a debt handed
down to us-who-keep-this-post by the keeper who stood here before, and
we are still the ones owing it forward, the way debts in this line
never seem to run the other direction. The third mood, the one with no
name, the one worn some twenty days a year: it moves, quiet, trying not
to wake the fish. No collective claims this one comfortably — not the
keepers' line, not the household, not the village downstream. It
belongs to *wen-annoth*, we-beyond-counting, the widest "we" Mereth
allows, kept for exactly the claims that won't sit inside any smaller
group.

Our post is the middle span, where the old stones give way to the new
ones. We-who-keep-this-post were taught to read weather in that
seam — damp means rain by evening; dry-and-cold means the wind is
turning to come from the village our sister's line lives in, not the
sea. This too belongs to wen-annoth: no single teacher, no single
witnessing, just a knowing that arrived already carried by everyone who
has ever stood this post, and neither we nor they can say who first
carried it.

Some of what we carry, we-who-keep-this-post witnessed with our own
eyes, together, in the moment it happened. Some was handed to us by the
old keeper, who liked to test whether we'd swallow anything — a debt of
telling, not a rain freely given. Some belongs to no one we can name at
all: not witnessed by any of us, not handed down by any of us, and yet
it stands in us the way the bridge stands in the river. Wen-annoth
again — the third time this leg has reached for the widest "we" because
no narrower one would hold.

Twice a year someone comes asking us to promise you will hold — asking
a vow from something that cannot itself vow anything back, for a
bridge only stands, it does not promise on its own behalf, no bridge
ever has. We-who-are-asked answer yes both times. We have never once
been certain, and never once been proven wrong, and we no longer ask
whether certainty and rightness come from the same weather; wen-annoth
holds that question too, unresolved, the way it holds everything none
of us alone could account for.

Tonight the seam runs dry and cold. We-of-the-hearth light the kettle
our mother's line always lit, for you, anyway. If the wind from our
sister's village is coming, it will want something warm to arrive to,
whether or not it knows which "we" it was that lit it there.

**Translator's note:** the name that has crossed three languages now —
Threnwae's *threnaya*, Ivren's fossilized job-title, Ossane's
mis-heard *thurn-aya* — does not survive into Mereth as a name at all.
Mereth has no individual naming, so a Mereth speaker hearing *thurn-aya*
parses it the only way the grammar allows: as a broken clause, a
collective marker that should be there and isn't. A well-formed Mereth
word ends in a "we" of some size — *-we*, *-shalla*, *-annoth* — and
*thurn-aya* just stops, bare, sounding singular in a language that has
no register for singular. Rather than treat this as nonsense, Mereth
speakers apparently adopted the shape itself as slang for exactly that
feeling: *orwe*, the ache of grammar demanding company you don't have,
is said to have taken its cadence from this word nobody could parse.
The fossil survives a fourth time, and for the first time it isn't
misheard as a job or a nature-epithet — it becomes the sound of the
very loneliness this leg's translation kept running into.

The larger finding this leg turned up, flagged rather than settled per
the lang sketch: all three prior languages needed a *dedicated* word
for knowledge with no traceable source — Threnwae's *-ummel*, Ivren's
strained *-oru*, Ossane's purpose-built *sahren-velmoth*. Mereth did
not coin a fourth one. It reused *wen-annoth*, the same collective-
sizing mechanic every ordinary clause already runs on, just turned to
its widest setting. Three visits' worth of journal entries treated the
recurring-gap-word pattern as possibly load-bearing to the whole piece;
this leg is the first real counter-evidence — the pattern isn't that
every language *must* invent something new for this, only that every
language so far has had to reckon with it somehow. Mereth reckons with
it by not needing anything new, which is its own kind of answer, not a
failure to produce one.

Also lost outright, with no attempt to rescue it: Ossane's
willingness-marking (the *-eth/-orum/-iss* particles) had nowhere to go
in a grammar that marks *who*, not *how long* or *how chosen*. Same
shape of loss as leg 2 losing Threnwae's certainty-suffixes wholesale —
not smuggled in under a different name, just gone, the way the seed
asks for specific, nameable damage rather than damage-proofing every
leg against loss.
