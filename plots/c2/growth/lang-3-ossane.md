# Ossane

*a living sketch, extended over future visits*

Ossane has no ordinary verbs. Every finite verb in the language conjugates
from one of seven weather-roots, and to say anything that happens — an
action, a feeling, a fact holding true — a speaker must first decide which
of the seven weathers it most resembles:

- *seffi* — rains. Slow, willing, repeated: tending, giving, going on
  loving something out of habit rather than heat.
- *thurn* — stills. Presence without motion: waiting, dwelling, keeping
  watch, simply being somewhere. Ossane's closest thing to a verb "to be."
- *korrast* — gusts. Sudden and involuntary: being struck by a feeling,
  a fact landing all at once, witnessing something with your own eyes in
  a single moment rather than learning it over time.
- *velmoth* — thaws. Release, change of state: forgiving, giving something
  up, a quiet transition nobody forced.
- *hessk* — freezes. Refusal, compulsion, a debt or a binding that has not
  resolved and may never: owing, being owed, being bound to a post you
  didn't choose.
- *ombrant* — floods. Being overwhelmed: grief, excess, losing the shape
  of your own intentions to something bigger.
- *sahren* — dries. Absence: lack, running out, a waiting that has been
  emptied of the thing it was waiting for.

Every verb takes a duration/will particle after the root — *-eth* (briefly),
*-orum* (for years, past the point anyone would ask it of you), *-iss*
(against your own will) — so a speaker cannot just name the weather, they
must also confess how long they've stood in it and whether they chose to.

**What it loses:** any verb that isn't, structurally, also a weather
report. Ivren's four-way split of belonging — tends, dwells-with, owes,
binds-to — does not map cleanly onto seven roots that were never built to
carry it; some of those distinctions collapse into the same root, and the
collapse is not a translation failure so much as Ossane's honest opinion
that the distinction didn't matter as much as Ivren thought. Precision
between *kinds* of action goes; in its place, precision about duration and
willingness, which neither Threnwae nor Ivren marks at all.

**What it imposes:** the admission that doing is also weathering — you
cannot act in Ossane without simultaneously describing the atmospheric
condition you are undergoing while you do it. There is no neutral
"is" either; existing is *thurn*, stilling, so merely holding a post
(a bridge-keeper, say) already commits a speaker to the language's
stillness-verb whether or not it feels still to live.

**Untranslatable words (so far):**

- *seffi-orum* — a caring continued so long past the point of being asked
  for that it has stopped registering as effort at all; the specific
  non-feeling of a rain that has fallen for years and is no longer
  weather to the person standing in it, just climate.
- *sahren-velmoth* — literally "a dry thaw": a fact or change that arrives
  having done its work with no visible rain that could have caused it.
  Every one of these languages, it turns out, needs a word for knowledge
  that shows up without a door — Threnwae calls it *-ummel*, Ivren forces
  *-oru* to cover it uneasily. This is Ossane's answer, and unlike the
  other two it isn't a grammatical suffix or a borrowed verb doing extra
  work — it's its own compound, built for exactly this and nothing else.

**A question the translation keeps raising, not yet settled:** the source
material is soaked in literal weather (a river with moods, a seam that
predicts rain) landing in a language where weather is also the entire
grammar. Leg 3 lets literal and grammatical weather touch in a few places
— the river's actual flood-mood rendered with the same root, *ombrant*,
that would mark any ordinary overwhelm. Whether Ossane speakers actually
experience that collision — whether they even have a boundary between
"describing weather" and "using the weather-grammar," the way English
speakers can always tell a metaphor from a forecast — or whether this is
the translator reading a pun into a coincidence, is genuinely unresolved.
Flagging it rather than deciding it.

**Agentive split (resolved):** tested the will-requiring five (*seffi*,
*korrast*, *hessk*, *velmoth*, *ombrant*) against every verb leg 3 gives
the bridge, the kettle, and the river — not just the one sentence
("you do not rain or freeze on your own behalf, no bridge does") that
first needed the rule. The bridge and the kettle never once take a
will-requiring root on their own behalf anywhere in the leg; every
*seffi* or *hessk* near them belongs to whoever tends or reads them; the
things themselves only ever *thurn* or *sahren*. The river looks like a
counterexample at first — it *floods* (*ombrant*) what it wants and *is
frozen* (*hessk*) owing a debt, both on its own behalf, with no person
doing the flooding or the owing for it — but that isn't the split
breaking, it's the split holding on a category nobody had named yet. The
rule was never "people versus objects"; it's *willful versus willless*,
and the river has been willful since before Ossane existed — the source
text already gives it the only two active verbs ("it says nothing and
takes what it wants") anything but the keeper gets in the whole diary.
Ossane inherited a river with will and a bridge without one; the seven
roots just made that difference legible for the first time.

**Left for later visits:** Threnwae's still-open plural-address question
is not Ossane's to answer, and this visit didn't try.
