# Torvane

*a living sketch, extended over future visits*

Every finite verb in Torvane carries a mandatory causation marker, one of
three: *-esk* (self-caused — the act arose from the actor's own will or
nature), *-oth* (other-caused — the act happened to the actor because of
someone or something else's doing), or *-nirel* (uncaused — the act simply
occurred, with no agent behind it a speaker can name, not even the actor's
own will). There is no fourth option and no way to leave the question
unmarked; an unmarked verb is not a finished clause in Torvane, the same
rule Threnwae set for its own certainty-suffixes.

**What it loses:** the ability to describe an action while staying
agnostic about where it came from. A Torvane speaker can never simply
report that something happened — she must also rule, every time, on
whether it was willed, imposed, or sourceless, even for acts so habitual
their origin has gone invisible even to the one performing them.

**What it imposes:** causal verdict as a condition of speech, and with it
a vertigo Threnwae's evidentiality never had to risk. *-orin/-adel/-ummel*
ask only how you learned a thing — a question about the past, settled
once answered. *-esk/-oth/-nirel* ask what set a thing in motion — a claim
about the world that new evidence can overturn. A woman who has marked her
own kettle-keeping *-esk* her whole life can discover, mid-sentence, that
it was *-oth* all along — her mother's hand still on it, decades on.

**Untranslatable words (so far):**

- *velnesh* — the vertigo of re-marking a long-held *-esk* act as *-oth*,
  or *-oth* as *-esk*, once new evidence about its true source arrives;
  the ground moving under a claim you never thought to check.
- *tarrunin* — an act performed so habitually that its cause has gone
  invisible even to the one who does it; not *-nirel* (there is a cause,
  somewhere) and not comfortably *-esk* either (she no longer feels
  herself choosing it) — Torvane speakers mark it *-esk* anyway, by
  convention, and *tarrunin* names the specific unease of that convention,
  not the act itself.

**What happens to the inherited mechanics:** unlike the last two
crossings, this one is not a total loss. Naveth's shape-particles had
nowhere to attach once Vendrel reoriented grammar around affirm-or-silence
instead of who's-addressed — a structural incompatibility, not a choice.
Torvane's causation-marking sits on a different axis entirely — it asks
what set an act in motion, not whether a clause affirms or falls silent —
and nothing about that axis crowds out Vendrel's own. So Torvane keeps
Vendrel's *-arden* (replacement clause) and *sohu* (the silence break)
intact, layered under its own mandatory causation mark: a denial still
needs a replacement or a silence, and now that replacement or silence also
needs its own causation verdict. This is the first inherited mechanic in
the chain to survive a full crossing instead of being emptied out — not
because I decided in advance that this one should survive, but because I
checked this crossing for the same kind of hard incompatibility that
killed the last two and didn't find one. Worth flagging plainly, since
visit 12 asked the question on purpose: the losses were never a rule of
the chain, they were particular to what each new axis happened to crowd
out.

**A revived thread, and for the first time not straining to arrive:** the
sourceless-knowledge progression — Threnwae's suffix, Ivren's strained
borrowing, Ossane's purpose-built compound, Mereth's reuse of its own
widest mechanic, Naveth's total absence, Vendrel's forced silence-break —
gets a seventh installment here, and this is the first one that costs
Torvane nothing to produce. Causation-marking simply *has* an uncaused
case built in from the start; a fact with no traceable source is not an
edge case Torvane has to strain, borrow, or force a door open for, the
way every predecessor did in its own way. *-nirel* was always going to be
there, waiting for exactly this.

**Resolved (visit 16):** both threads flagged above turned out to be one
thread. Leg 7's kettle passage revised to stage the vertigo live — the
keeper, mid-sentence, feels *velnesh* pull the kettle-keeping toward
*-oth* (her dead mother's hand) and does not let it land there, does not
let it fall back to *-esk* either, and marks *-nirel* on purpose instead:
a marker chosen deliberately is only legible as chosen, rather than
merely correct, against a moment where a plausible alternative was
genuinely on the table. *Tarrunin* is what made the moment available —
an act already felt as habit rather than choice is exactly where the
vertigo has somewhere real to pull toward.

**Left for later visits:** whether declining the choice, once, becomes
itself worn into a second habit — a *tarrunin* around the refusal to
resolve, rather than around the keeping — is untested; the leg stages the
decline exactly once, with no second occasion to check whether it repeats
the same way or differently. This remains genuinely open: testing it
would need a second scene where the same speaker declines a choice again,
and nothing in the source's five paragraphs stages one — the same kind of
gap that has kept Naveth's reverse-vertigo question parked since visit 9,
not a thread anyone has actually taken up yet.

The other half — whether an eighth language reopens a naming or address
system that could give *threnaya* somewhere to land — is answered as of
visit 17, inside `lang-8-vaelith.md` and leg 8: Vaelith has exactly such a
system, and the fossil still doesn't land, but for a new reason (the chain
rule, not a missing grammar) rather than the old one. See `index.md` for
the full account.
