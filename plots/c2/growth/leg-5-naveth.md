# Leg 5 — through Naveth

*rendered into English from a Naveth original, itself translated from the
Mereth of leg 4, not from Ossane, Ivren, Threnwae, or the source before
them — the chain still moves one link at a time. Naveth marks nothing of
the speaker (Mereth's collective-subject apparatus has nowhere to attach
in a grammar built around the listener, not the speaker, and does not
survive); every second-person reference below carries instead a
shape-particle for its listener: -sol (one bonded listener, capacity to
answer not required), -fal (a settled, mutually-known throng), -erin
(open, unsecured, thrown toward whoever or no one).*

I keep you-sol the way my mother kept her kettle: lit, always, whether or
not anyone was coming for tea. Six years now. Below, the river holds
three moods, and I have come to know all three, though which is honest I
cannot say — that would need a fourth mood, one for telling the truth
about the other three, and the river has never handed me that one.

In flood it says nothing and takes what it wants. In drought it
complains, all afternoon, about a debt someone owes it — a debt handed
down to me by the keeper who stood here before, and I am still the one
owing it forward, the way this debt never seems to run the other
direction. The third mood, the one with no name, the one worn some
twenty days a year: it moves, quiet, trying not to wake the fish. No one
taught me to claim this one and I do not know whose it is — the mood
you-sol wear that has never once needed an owner.

My post is the middle span, where the old stones give way to the new
ones. I was taught to read weather in that seam — damp means rain by
evening; dry-and-cold means the wind is turning to come from my sister's
village, not the sea. I have never checked this and it does not ask to
be checked; it arrived already true, and I cannot say who first carried
it to me, only that someone did.

Some of what I carry I witnessed with my own eyes. Some was handed to me
by the old keeper, who liked to test whether I'd swallow anything. Some
belongs to no one I can name at all — not witnessed, not told, and yet
it stands in me the way you-sol stand in the river. I have stopped
asking where it came from. Naveth gives me no word for that question and
I have stopped needing one.

Twice a year the village comes asking me to promise you-sol will hold —
asking a vow from something that cannot itself vow anything back, for a
bridge only stands, it does not promise on its own behalf, no bridge ever
has. I answer them yes-fal, both times, the whole gathered weight of
them at once, not one villager averaged into another. I have never once
been certain, and never once been proven wrong, and I no longer
think-erin, thrown to no one, not even to you, that certainty and
rightness come from the same weather.

Tonight the seam runs dry and cold. I light the kettle my mother always
lit, for you-sol, anyway. If my sister's wind is coming, it will want
something warm to arrive to, whether or not it knows that it was only
ever me, alone, who lit it there for you-sol.

**Translator's note:** the loudest loss this leg makes is Mereth's own —
every "we-who-keep-this-post," every careful sizing of household against
lineage against *wen-annoth*, collapses back into a bare "I" the moment
the grammar's attention turns from *who is speaking* to *who is spoken
to*. This is a starker version of a loss the chain has made before (leg
3 dropping Ivren's relation-verbs, leg 4 dropping Ossane's willingness-
marking): not a new mechanism replacing an old one, but no mechanism at
all where Mereth had built its richest one. The sourceless-knowledge
question that needed a suffix in Threnwae, a strained borrowing in
Ivren, a purpose-built compound in Ossane, and Mereth's own widest "we" —
here it needs nothing. It just gets said, flatly, the same as any other
fact. Whether that is the pattern finally dying or the pattern's truest
form (an assumption every language before Naveth was, in its own way,
refusing to make) is worth a later visit's distance, not this one's.

The name that has crossed four languages now — Threnwae's *threnaya*,
Ivren's frozen job-title, Ossane's misheard *thurn-aya*, Mereth's broken-
clause slang for *orwe* — arrives in Naveth as *thurnai* and finds, for
the first time, a grammar built to notice something specific about it:
Naveth requires every clause to carry a shape-particle for its listener,
and *thurnai* carries none. Not broken, the way Mereth heard it — Naveth
has no clause-final expectation the way Mereth did, so there's no seam
for the word to fail at. It simply sits outside the address system
entirely, the way a fixed title does. Naveth speakers use it now for
exactly that: whatever keeps its function regardless of being addressed
or addressing anyone back — on, always, whether or not anyone is coming
for tea. The fossil's fifth crossing is the first to land on the
source's own opening image rather than drift from it, though nothing
about the translation was aimed there on purpose; the languages found it
on their own, four visits apart.

Also worth flagging, per `lang-5-naveth.md`: this is the chain's first
real engagement with the plural-address question Threnwae's own sketch
left open in visit 1. *-fal*, used above for the village asking its
yearly promise, addresses a crowd as one settled unit rather than
averaging across its members — a live answer to the question, though a
Naveth answer, not a Threnwae one. Threnwae's own version of the
question is exactly as open as it was four visits ago; nothing here
closes it, only stands next to it.
