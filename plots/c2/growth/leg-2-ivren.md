# Leg 2 — through Ivren

*rendered into English from an Ivren original, itself translated from
the Threnwae of leg 1, not from the English source — the chain moves
one link at a time. Ivren has no relational self-naming, so the speaker
uses a plain first person; but* threnaya *survives as a frozen loanword,
a job title with the grammar that made it long gone.*

Threnaya tends you the way she tends the kettle her mother tends
(*aroth vash-eni*) — going, always, whether or not anyone is coming for
tea. Six years now that she is bound to this. Below, the river keeps
three moods, and she has come to know all three, though which of the
three is honest she cannot say — that would need a fourth mood, one for
telling the truth about the other three, and the river owes her that
mood and has never paid it.

In flood it says nothing and takes what it wants. In drought it
complains, all afternoon, about a debt someone owes it — a debt she was
handed by the keeper who dwelt this post before her, and now owes it
forward in turn, unpaid, the way debts in this family only ever move
one direction. The third mood, the one with no name, the one it wears
some twenty days a year: it moves, quiet, like it's trying not to wake
the fish. This one she cannot explain how she came to know; it is not
tended or dwelt-with or owed, it simply arrived already hers to carry,
which Ivren has no clean verb for and calls, uneasily, *-oru* anyway,
as if a fact could be something you cohabit with.

Her post is the middle span, where the old stones give way to the new
ones — a seam she is bound to reading, worn into her the way weather
wears a threshold: damp means rain by evening; dry-and-cold means the
wind is turning to come from the village her sister dwells in instead
of from the sea. This she carries the way one carries *revka* — still
tending a knowledge she is no longer sure needs her tending, and
choosing to tend it anyway, because to stop would say something she
does not mean to say.

Some of what threnaya carries she came to by her own two eyes. Some was
handed to her by the old keeper, who liked to test whether she'd
swallow anything — a debt of a stranger kind, knowledge owed rather
than given outright. Some she cannot account for at all: it is not
tended, not dwelt-with, not owed, and yet it stands in her the way the
bridge stands in the river, so she calls it *-oru* and lets the word do
work it was not quite built for.

Twice a year someone comes asking her to bind you to a promise you
cannot yourself make — for you are *senket-oru*, dwelt-with, not a
person who can owe or be owed — and she answers yes both times, the way
one accepts a debt on another's behalf. She has never once been
certain, and never once been proven wrong, and no longer holds these
two facts as related; she only holds them, the way she holds everything
now, as things she tends without asking whether tending was ever asked
of her.

Tonight the seam is dry and cold. Threnaya puts the kettle her mother
tends on for you anyway. If the wind her sister dwells in is coming, it
will want something warm to arrive to, whether or not it knows who kept
it waiting.

---

**Translator's note:** the hardest casualty crossing from Threnwae into
Ivren was the certainty-suffix — *-orin/-adel/-ummel* has no Ivren
equivalent, because Ivren doesn't ask *how you know something*, only
*what kind of hold it has on you*. Every place leg 1 confessed witnessed,
told, or dreamed, leg 2 had to substitute a diagnosis of tending,
dwelling, owing, or binding instead — which meant some sentences
changed meaning on the way through, not just texture. The dreamed
facts (*-ummel*) were the worst fit: Ivren has no verb for a thing that
arrived without a door, so I forced *-oru* (dwells-with) onto them,
which is a slight lie the language is willing to tell rather than admit
a gap. *Velk* did not survive at all — Ivren already treats "asking a
bridge to hold" as ordinary debt-logic (*senket-oru* dwelling, not
owing, so the promise-extraction is recast as binding a debt onto
oneself on the bridge's behalf) — the specific unease Threnwae named is
still visible in the seams but no longer has its own word. *Threnaya*
made it across whole, but hollowed: a name that used to shift with its
addressee now just sits there, a fossil of a grammar Ivren never had.
