# Leg 8 — through Vaelith

*rendered into English from a Vaelith original, itself translated from
the Torvane of leg 7, not from Vendrel, Naveth, Mereth, Ossane, Ivren,
Threnwae, or the source before them — the chain still moves one link at
a time. Vaelith inherits Torvane's three causation marks whole (*-esk*
self-caused, *-oth* other-caused, *-nirel* uncaused) and puts them to a
second job: no referent may be named by a bare noun or pronoun. Every
person, place, or thing must be addressed by an epithet built from the
one act the speaker has witnessed it do or undergo, fused with the
causation mark for whose doing that act was — or, where nothing
witnessed yet earns a rooted name, by the placeholder *kelvet*,
not-yet-witnessed.*

Vessa-nirel keeps the kettle lit — vessa-nirel, the-one-who-keeps, cause
declined, and this leg can only build her one way: the last thing
witnessed of her specific enough to root a name in is the moment she
looked straight at her own keeping and would not say whose hand was
still on it. Vessa-esk kept it before her — her mother, the-one-who-kept,
cause claimed outright, no vertigo in that naming, because none is on
record; the act was witnessed once, plain, and the epithet holds it
plain, the way vessa-nirel's does not. Whether anyone aesa-nirel — the
one who arrives, uncaused, meaning here whoever might come for tea — or
that coming stands empty, unfilled by a single guest, year on year, is
no part of either woman's name. Vaelith names a person by what was seen
of her, not by what does or doesn't arrive after her.

Below, sohven-esk wears three moods — the river, the-one-who-carries-
weather, self-caused, the only referent in this whole telling whose
epithet needed no second thought, since nothing has ever stood behind
its weather but itself. In flood sohven-esk takes what it wants and
answers no one. In drought sohven-esk complains, all afternoon, of a
debt aksa-esk says it is owed — the village, the-ones-who-ask,
self-caused, since asking is their own act however the debt itself
came to be. The third mood has no witnessed act sharp enough to root —
not because nothing happens, but because nothing that happens has ever
been specific enough to tell apart from any other twenty quiet days —
so it stands as kelvet, not-yet-witnessed, moving quiet, minding the
fish, unnamed the way it has been unnamed in every telling before this
one, though never before by exactly this reasoning. Three languages now
have reached for the same six words in the source and answered them
three different ways: a compound built to hold what has no traceable
cause, a replacement clause standing in for the name never given, and
now a plain admission that a name was never earned, not that one is
owed and missing.

Vessa-nirel's post is kesta-esk — the bridge, the-one-who-holds,
self-caused, for a bridge answers-nirel for nothing on its own behalf,
no bridge ever has, nothing behind its holding but its own standing,
cause enough by itself — at the middle span, where old stones give way
to new. The seam shows itself to anyone who knows to look; yenna-esk
taught vessa-nirel to read weather there — the keeper before her,
the-one-who-taught, self-caused, his own doing entirely, the same man
who liked to test what she'd swallow. Vessa-nirel holds this as true.
Whether she has ever checked it: kelvet, not-yet-witnessed even to
herself, since a fact you have never tested cannot earn its checker a
name for testing it. Some of what she carries she witnessed with these
eyes, sohven-esk's or kesta-esk's doing, the world's own; some yenna-esk
handed her, his doing entirely; and some arrived with nothing behind it
she can root a giver's name in at all — not aesa-nirel's doing, not
anyone's, no moment she can point to where it became hers. She can only
call it what it is: kelvet, not because no cause exists somewhere, but
because nothing about its arrival was ever witnessed sharply enough to
build a name from.

Twice a year aksa-esk comes asking a promise of kesta-esk — the village
asking, self-caused, of the bridge that holds, self-caused, one
epithet addressing another, neither borrowing the other's causation.
Vessa-nirel answers yes, both times, her own doing, freely given — for
this act, unlike the kettle, she does not decline; nothing about saying
yes to the asking has ever come to her as anything but her own. Her
certainty has stood absent every time she has reached for it, and
rightness has stood beside her all the same, answerable to no cause she
can name either way. These two she once held as one thing, sharing a
root; she holds them now as strangers to each other, needing nothing
between them to go on being true — not a fact about kesta-esk or
aksa-esk, and so not a fact this leg's naming-rule has anything to say
about. Vaelith names referents, not the shape of a woman's own
uncertainty; some things in this diary stay outside the rule entirely,
and this is one.

Tonight the seam runs dry and cold. Vessa-nirel lights the kettle
anyway, the same worn keeping this ledger opened with — sevrasti, a
little, in the writing of it: the last witnessed act naming her is
still the declining, not the keeping itself, though the keeping is what
her hands are doing right now, this line, as the name is being read. If
aesa-nirel is coming, warmth will be waiting for it, whether it knows
yenna-esk's daughter lit it there or only finds it already burning — a
warmth that asks nothing of whoever, if anyone, it is for, the same
indifference vessa-esk's kettle always kept and vessa-nirel's still
does, whatever she now calls the keeping.

**Translator's note:** the naming-rule costs this leg something none of
Torvane's causation-marking on verbs alone could — a referent that
Vaelith simply cannot yet name honestly stays *kelvet* in the sentence
itself, visible as an admission rather than smoothed over the way a
bare pronoun would smooth it in English. The river is still the
telling's one purely self-caused thing, now doubly so: its every verb
was already *-esk* in Torvane, and its very name, *sohven-esk*, is
*-esk* too, the first referent in eight legs whose epithet needed no
deliberation at all. Vessa-nirel and vessa-esk, mother and daughter,
share a root and differ only in mark — the closest this chain has come
to putting two epithets side by side that make the reader do the
comparing Torvane's own translator's note only described in prose.

Vaelith is the return of a real "I," seven languages after Threnwae
first refused one — but earned by an entirely different road. Threnwae
asked *who are you to your listener*; Vaelith asks *what has anyone
actually seen you do*. Both refuse a name that simply persists on its
own; neither will let a speaker fall back on being generically herself.
Worth naming as a real rhyme rather than a coincidence: this is the
first language since the first to make self-naming genuinely
effortful, and it does so from the opposite direction.

The fossil name does not appear in this leg, for a third leg running,
and for a reason distinct from the last two — see `lang-8-vaelith.md`'s
own note. Vessa-nirel is not *threnaya* returning; it is a wholly
separate root, earned fresh from this leg's own witnessed material,
that happens to land in the same shape a name built from a keeping-verb
and a cause-mark always was going to have. The parallel is real and
worth sitting with, but it is a re-arrival at a shape, not a recovery
of a word.
