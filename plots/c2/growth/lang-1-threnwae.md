# Threnwae

*a living sketch, extended over future visits*

Threnwae is spoken by people who never developed a word that means only
"I." You do not name yourself in the abstract; you name yourself in
relation to whoever you're speaking to, or to whatever you're speaking
toward. A parent talking to a child calls herself *nemet* — the-one-who-
came-before. That same woman talking to her own mother calls herself
*sowen* — the-one-who-came-after. Talking to a bridge, a river, a house —
anything without a family tree — a speaker calls herself by her post:
*threnaya*, the-one-who-keeps.

**What it loses:** the fixed self. There is no word to fall back on when
you don't know your relation to the listener — a Threnwae speaker
addressing a stranger must first work out what they are to each other
before the sentence can finish. Interior monologue, spoken to no one, is
grammatically unfinished; Threnwae has no comfortable way to talk to
itself.

**What it imposes:** certainty-marking on every finite verb. Threnwae
verbs take one of three suffixes, and a sentence without one isn't a
sentence:

- *-orin* — witnessed. You saw it, heard it, felt it happen.
- *-adel* — told. Someone else gave you this, and you are passing it on
  without claiming it.
- *-ummel* — dreamed. You have it, whole and certain-feeling, and you
  cannot say where you got it. Threnwae does not treat this as a lesser
  kind of knowing — *-ummel* is not "unreliable," it is its own register,
  used for the things a person is most sure of and least able to source.

A Threnwae speaker literally cannot assert a plain fact. Every claim
comes stamped with its own provenance, whether the speaker wants to
disclose that or not.

**Untranslatable words (so far):**

- *seam-orin* — a fact you learned by watching it happen with your own
  eyes, so many times it became furniture. Stronger than "I know," softer
  than "I saw."
- *velk* — the specific unease of being promised to by something that
  cannot itself promise anything back (a bridge, a kettle, a season).
  There's no verb "to promise" in Threnwae that a non-person can take;
  when a bridge is asked to hold, the asker is said to *velk* it, not to
  extract a vow from it.

**The verb "to keep":** root *thren-*, and it explains its own epithet —
*threnaya* is *thren-* plus the agentive *-aya*, so "the-one-who-keeps"
was never a separate coinage, it's the verb wearing a suffix. But the
root is stricter than English "keep": bare *thren-* is not a word.
Keeping in Threnwae is grammatically incapable of being a one-off — the
root must fuse with the continuative *-esh* (*thren-esh*, "kept, going
on, regardless of whether called for") before it can take a certainty
suffix and become a real verb. There is no way to say "I kept it that
one time"; the moment keeping stops being indifferent to use, Threnwae
simply has no verb for what you're describing anymore — some other word
would have to do, and none of the ones this diary needed ever came up.
This is the same mechanic as *velk* wearing a different face: both
words refuse to let "for someone" or "on this occasion" attach to an act
of tending. A Threnwae speaker cannot describe conditional care at all.

**Plural address (resolved):** the relation-naming was only ever built
for a single listener — *nemet* to a child, *sowen* to a parent,
*threnaya* to anything without a family tree. A crowd breaks the
mechanic the moment it isn't made of one relation: a village gathered
to ask a favor might hold the speaker's own child, her own mother, and
forty people she owes nothing in particular, all listening at once, and
Threnwae has no relation-word that means "child-to-me and stranger-to-
me simultaneously," nor any rule for averaging a mixed audience into
one name.

The answer doesn't need a new mechanism, only the one Threnwae already
has: a speaker facing an audience whose relation to her isn't uniform
treats that audience the way she'd treat a bridge or a river — as
something without one family-tree relation to name herself against —
and falls back to her post-name. A crowd of *confirmed single* relation
(an address to "my children," all of them actually her children) still
takes the ordinary relation-word, used collectively with no separate
plural marker; Threnwae speakers rely on context to know whether
*nemet* names one child or a room of them. The fallback only engages
when the crowd's relation to the speaker is mixed, unknown, or
unverifiable — which, for most speakers facing most crowds, is most of
the time. The rule was never "average across the crowd"; it's "a crowd
is one more kind of thing without a family tree, whenever you can't be
sure it's made of just one relation" — and Threnwae already had a word
for that category before anyone thought to ask about crowds.

**Left for later visits:** whether Threnwae has a future tense at all,
or only ever speaks of the not-yet as a kind of *-ummel* — untouched,
still fully open.
