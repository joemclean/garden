# Leg 10 — through Threshel

*rendered into English from a Threshel original, itself translated from
the Calvorn of leg 9, not from Vaelith, Torvane, Vendrel, Naveth, Mereth,
Ossane, Ivren, Threnwae, or the source before them — the chain still
moves one link at a time. Threshel mints no new names of its own; every
referent here is a name leg 8 settled and leg 9 carried forward whole —
vessa-nirel, vessa-esk, sohven-esk, kesta-esk, yenna-esk, aksa-esk,
aesa-nirel, kelvet. What Threshel adds is a mark on every finite verb
declaring its position against whatever edge the speaker nominates for
that clause: *-imre* (this side), *-oswin* (the far side), or *-kelvra*
(astride the edge itself, or the edge being crossed).*

Vessa-nirel keeps-*kelvra* the kettle lit. Not nearside, in the warm room
where it sits, and not farside, out on the bridge she actually watches —
the keeping itself stands astride the one line the act has always
refused to fall on either side of: whether tonight belongs to someone
coming or to no one at all. Vessa-esk kept it the same way before her,
also *-kelvra*, for the same reason; a threshold doesn't stop being one
because the woman standing on it changes. Whether her mother's keeping
ever, once, actually lapsed is a question Threshel answers differently
than Calvorn did: not how many times it broke, but which side of the
known/unknown line the claim itself stands on. Vessa-nirel's own six
years of keeping mark *-imre* — nearside, inside what she witnessed with
her own hands. Her mother's keeping marks *-oswin* — farside, on the
other side of a line vessa-nirel never personally crossed to check,
carried to her only as told word. She feels the old vertigo here too,
the same one that unsettled her mother's naming one leg back: not
whether the far side holds what it's supposed to, but whether she has
any standing at all to place it there rather than admitting she cannot
say which side it belongs on. She marks it *-oswin* anyway, because
"uncrossed" and "false" are not the same claim, and Threshel's grammar
only asks which side a speaker's own witness reaches, never whether the
far side is honest. Whether aesa-nirel is coming, tonight or any night,
gets no mark at all — an act that has not yet happened has no side to
stand on, because nothing has crossed into being yet for a threshold to
measure; *aesa-nirel* stays a name, the third language running to find
no clause can hold it, each for its own reason.

Below, sohven-esk wears three moods, and each sits differently against
the bank that is its most obvious edge. In flood it comes-*oswin* — over
the bank, onto the near ground, taking what it wants there, the one mood
that actually crosses. In drought it stays-*imre*, low in its own bed,
and complains from there, all afternoon, of a debt aksa-esk says it is
owed — the complaint travels, but the river itself never leaves its own
side to make it. The third mood, the one with no name in nine languages
running, is the one Threshel's own grammar was built for: it is
*-kelvra* by nature, moving so quietly along the line between bank and
water that neither side notices the crossing has happened at all, "like
it's trying not to wake the fish" — a mood that is astride not because
no one has named it, the way *kelvet* holds it in Vaelith, but because
its whole character is to occupy the edge itself and refuse to resolve
onto either side of it. Two structurally different reasons for the same
unnamed thing, four languages apart, neither aware of the other.

Yenna-esk taught-*kelvra* vessa-nirel to read the seam. Teaching is the
one act in this leg that Threshel marks *-kelvra* without hesitation,
whatever boundary is nominated: knowledge crossing from one side (the
teacher's own witnessed years) to the other (a girl who had none yet) is
the crossing itself, not a thing that happens on either bank of it.
Everything since is vessa-nirel reading the seam on her own, and every
one of those readings marks *-imre* — nearside, her own perception, her
own hand on the stone. Whether she has ever tested that reading against
what the weather actually does is a different question than Calvorn's
*-heth* asked, though it lands on the same six words: has she ever
crossed to the far side — the side where the wind either does or doesn't
turn the way the dry seam predicted — to check her nearside reading
against it? She hasn't, not once in six years; every reading has stayed
where it started, *-imre*, unconfronted by the *-oswin* that would prove
or break it. Vaelith called this gap *kelvet*, a referent too dim to
root a name in. Calvorn called it *-heth*, a history with nothing in it.
Threshel calls it a claim that has never once crossed to be checked
against its own far side — a third structurally distinct device, on a
sixth pass through the same source sentence, still not aware of the
other two.

Twice a year aksa-esk comes asking a promise of kesta-esk, and
vessa-nirel answers-*imre* — nearside, her own doing, freely given, no
farside required to make a yes a yes. Her certainty and her rightness
are a harder case: the source names them "two facts" the speaker no
longer thinks are related, and Threshel finds them sitting, structurally,
on opposite sides of a line neither the keeper nor the grammar can name
outright. Her uncertainty is *-imre* — hers, felt, nearside, the one
thing she can actually witness about her own promising. Her never having
been wrong is *-oswin* — a fact about outcomes, about the bridge's own
holding, farside of anything she can feel from where she stands; the
bridge's rightness is not hers to witness, only to receive news of.
Once, she held-*kelvra* the two as if they met at the same seam, the way
her post itself sits astride old stone and new. She no longer does. What
she holds now is that they may never have shared an edge at all —
*-imre* and *-oswin*, near and far, with no *-kelvra* actually joining
them, only the coincidence of her having stood at both for six years and
mistaken proximity for a shared threshold. Threshel has no word yet for
noticing two things were never really touching, only nearby; the closest
it can do is decline to mark them *-kelvra* any longer and say so
plainly.

Tonight the seam runs dry and cold, and reading it is, as always,
*-kelvra* — there is no other way to read a seam than by standing on it.
Vessa-nirel lights-*kelvra* the kettle anyway, the same threshold as the
leg's first line: not for someone, not for no one, astride the
difference exactly the way her keeping has always stood. Whether
aesa-nirel is coming stays what it has been all leg — no side, no mark,
a name waiting for a crossing that, if it happens at all, hasn't
happened yet.

**Translator's note:** Threshel's mandatory mark costs this leg
something none of the nine before it asked for directly: a running
survey of edges, not of knowledge, cause, or count. The seam itself —
literal in the source, the middle span "where the old stones give way to
the new ones" — turns out to be the one image nine prior languages
touched only as scenery and this one had to make its whole grammar out
of. Reading the seam was already, before any language touched it, an act
of standing at a threshold; Threshel is simply the first language forced
to say so on every verb, not just the ones about the bridge.

**On the sourceless-knowledge progression:** this is a sideways touch,
not a revival, and worth naming precisely rather than either claim.
Threshel's axis cannot ask *how* a fact arrived any more than Mereth's or
Naveth's could — that question still belongs to Threnwae, Ivren, and
Ossane alone. But it lands on the same material a sixth time, through a
different question again: not how vessa-nirel knows her mother's keeping
was unbroken, and not how many times it was taught, but which side of
the witnessed/unwitnessed line the claim itself falls on. A fourth
structurally distinct device now reaches the same six words in the
source ("I think this is true. I have never checked") without being
aware any of the other three exist.

**On the future-tense problem:** a third language now finds no way to
speak *aesa-nirel*'s arrival as a marked finite verb, and for a third
distinct reason. Threnwae had a door that happened to fit (*-ummel*,
sourceless, and the future is sourceless too). Calvorn had no door onto
the future at all, because all four of its marks audit a closed span.
Threshel has neither problem — its marks aren't about closure — and
still can't reach it, because an unrealized act hasn't crossed into
being yet, and a threshold-mark requires something to have a position
relative to an edge in the first place. Nothing to locate, not nowhere
to put it.

**Fossil, unchanged:** *threnaya* does not appear in this leg, for a
fifth leg running, for the same reason as the third and fourth: leg 9's
own text never carries the word forward, so leg 10 has nothing upstream
to find either.

**What happens to the inherited mechanics:** see `lang-10-threshel.md`'s
own accounting — Torvane's causation-marking and Vaelith's naming-rule
stay exactly as dormant here as they already were in Calvorn (nothing
new to name or cause, only old names to reuse); Calvorn's own
iteration-marking, by contrast, does not survive this crossing at all —
a genuine loss, checked rather than assumed, since frequency and
edge-position don't interact.
