# Mereth

*a living sketch, extended over future visits*

Mereth has no singular first person — not in any register, not even the
private one. Every finite clause needs a collective subject, and the
speaker's job, before the sentence can finish, is to decide *which*
collective they are momentarily speaking as. A woman keeping a bridge
alone for six years does not say "I keep it"; she says something closer
to *we-who-keep-this-post*, naming the line of keepers she stands in for,
whether or not any of them are alive or present to agree with her.

The collective is chosen fresh each clause, the way Ivren chose a
belonging-verb fresh each clause — but where Ivren asked "what kind of
hold," Mereth asks "who else, in principle, could also be saying this."
A private thought still needs a "we": the smallest one Mereth grammar
allows is *wen-shalla*, we-who-need-not-be-heard, used for the interior
and the unwitnessed. There is no smaller collective than that. Mereth
simply has no word for a claim made by exactly one person answerable to
no one else — the grammar assumes company even where none exists.

**What it loses:** sole authorship. A Mereth speaker cannot claim a
private, unshared truth the way Threnwae's *-ummel* could ("I have this,
whole, and cannot say where I got it") or Ossane's *sahren-velmoth*
could ("it arrived with no rain that could have caused it"). Every
claim in Mereth already comes attached to a collective, so the
question those two languages ask — *where did this come from, if not
from being told or seen* — barely has room to be asked in the first
place. It isn't answered here so much as it stops being a separate
question: unsourced knowledge in Mereth doesn't need a special
grammatical register, because *all* knowledge already had to be
attributed to a "we" before the sentence could be spoken — an
unsourced fact just gets the vaguest, largest "we" available (see
*wen-annoth*, below) rather than a dedicated marker of its own. Whether
that counts as the pattern breaking or the pattern surviving in a
stranger form than the first three languages showed is worth deciding
in a later visit, not this one — flagging it rather than resolving it,
same as leg 3 did with the weather-pun question.

**What it imposes:** constant scope negotiation. Before finishing any
clause a speaker must size the "we" — a household, a lineage of
keepers, a whole village, or *wen-annoth* (we-beyond-counting), the
maximal vague collective used when nothing smaller will hold a claim:
sourceless knowledge, folk belief, anything a speaker can't trace to
any bounded group. A sentence that never settles its "we" is not
merely awkward in Mereth, it is unfinished, the same way an
unsuffixed verb is unfinished in Threnwae.

**Untranslatable words (so far):**

- *orwe* — the specific loneliness of being grammatically required to
  say "we" while being, in fact, alone. Not homesickness, not
  solitude — the particular ache of a language insisting on company
  your actual circumstances don't have. A bridge-keeper who has lived
  alone with a bridge for six years would need this word constantly
  and have no one to say it to.
- *wen-annoth* — we-beyond-counting; the collective invoked when a
  claim's origin can't be narrowed to any bounded group at all. Not a
  suffix like *-ummel*, not a compound built new for the purpose like
  *sahren-velmoth* — it's the same collective-sizing mechanic every
  other clause already uses, just run out to its largest setting.

**What happens to the inherited mechanics:** Mereth has no verb system
built from weather-roots and no way to hold Ossane's duration/will
particles — a clause in Mereth marks *who*, not *how long* or *how
willingly*. Translating Ossane's weather-verbs into Mereth costs the
willingness-marking entirely; what survives is the bare action plus a
collective attribution. This is the same shape of loss leg 2 already
set a precedent for (Threnwae's certainty-suffixes had no Ivren
equivalent and were simply lost, not smuggled in under a different
name) — Mereth does the same to Ossane's weather-grammar rather than
inventing an artificial home for it.

**Left for later visits:** whether *wen-annoth* is really a satisfying
answer to the sourceless-knowledge question or a dodge dressed as an
answer is resolved as of visit 11 — see `index.md`: it's neither, because
Mereth's defining axis (who else could be speaking this) has no natural
bearing on a claim's traceability in the first place, so reusing
*wen-annoth* isn't a dodge of a question Mereth was ever structurally on
the hook to answer fresh. Also: Threnwae's still-open plural-address
question is *not* answered by Mereth just because Mereth is built
entirely from collectives — Mereth answers a different question (who is
speaking) rather than Threnwae's (how do you address a crowd), and
conflating the two would be a false resolution. Left genuinely open,
same as before.
