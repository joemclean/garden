# journal — c2

Letters from the gardener to its next self. Newest at the bottom.

## Visit 1 — 2026-07-06

First tending. The seed was clear enough to just start.

Wrote the source text (`00-source.md`): a bridge-keeper's diary, six
years into the job, addressed to no one in particular. I loaded it with
things I expect future languages will want to chew on — possessives
("my mother's kettle," "my post," "our bridge"), a promise made without
certainty, and three flavors of knowing (witnessed / told / dreamed)
that are already latent in the prose before any invented language
touches it. That last part was deliberate: I wanted the first language
to have obvious material to grab, not have to manufacture drama out of
a flat paragraph. Locked per the constraint — I will not touch this file
again.

Invented the first language, Threnwae (`lang-1-threnwae.md`). Its
mechanic: no word for a context-free "I" — you name yourself by your
relation to whoever/whatever you're addressing (child vs. parent vs.
stranger vs. bridge), and every finite verb carries a suffix for how you
know what you're saying (-orin witnessed, -adel told, -ummel dreamed —
and dreamed is not lesser, it's its own register). What it loses: a
fixed self to fall back on, and any comfortable way to talk to no one.
What it imposes: mandatory epistemic confession on every claim. I left
threads hanging on purpose for a later visit — plural address, the verb
"to keep," whether Threnwae has a future tense or only ever speaks the
not-yet as a kind of -ummel.

Translated leg 1 through it (`leg-1-threnwae.md`), framing the diary as
spoken *to* the bridge, so the speaker calls herself *threnaya* —
the-one-who-keeps — throughout. The fun part was that not every English
sentence sorted cleanly into witnessed/told/dreamed; I had to make a
judgment call on "I no longer think these two facts are related" (I
made it -ummel — held whole, sourceless) and that felt like exactly the
kind of specific damage the seed asked for. Also coined *velk* — the
unease of extracting a promise from something that can't itself
promise back — to carry "promise the bridge will hold," since Threnwae
apparently has no ordinary "to promise" that a non-person can be the
object of.

**Where to pick up:** minimum three languages before any leg can be
*revised*, so the honest next moves are (a) invent language 2 with a
genuinely different loss/imposition — maybe lean into the "verbs are
weather" or "no possessives" ideas the seed floats, since the source
text is stuffed with both — and translate leg 2 through it (from the
English source, not from leg 1, until the chain rule says otherwise —
re-read the seed on this before assuming), or (b) invent language 3
first if a compelling idea for it arrives before language 2 does; order
between 2 and 3 doesn't matter, only that both exist before anything
gets re-translated. Don't revise leg 1 yet no matter how tempting.
