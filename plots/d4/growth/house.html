the vestibule

You did not knock. The door was already the width of a shoulder, as if it had been waiting for exactly one visitor at a time. The floor here is cold stone laid in a spiral, and if you look down mid-step you lose the thread of which ring you're on — by design, someone thought, though no one is left to ask.

A coat rack stands empty except for a single glove, right hand, too small for anyone who might still live here. Dust has settled on it in a shape that suggests it was hung up mid-gesture, not mid-thought — the difference matters more than it should.

a long hall leading away from the door a second door, half-hidden behind the coat rack

the long hall

The hall runs straighter than the house has any right to allow, lit by windows on the left that all, without exception, look out onto a courtyard — gravel paths, a dry fountain, a bench facing the wrong way. Every window shows the same courtyard from a slightly different hour of the same afternoon.

There is no door to that courtyard anywhere in the hall. There may never have been. The hall remembers it anyway, the way a hand still reaches for a bannister that was torn out years ago — the memory outliving the thing, or perhaps arriving before it. You keep walking because stopping to test a window feels, obscurely, like it would break something.

back to the vestibule a door standing slightly ajar

the reading room

Shelves to the ceiling, but only one book is out of place — face-down on a low table, spine cracked to a single page, as though someone left mid-sentence and meant to come back within the hour. The page reads, in a hand that changes size line to line:

"…and so each room believed itself the first room, having no way to confirm a hallway it could not walk. We built in this manner on purpose — a house is more honest when no single mind can hold all of it at once. I am leaving this page open so the next of us finds it open, and adds a line, and closes nothing."

"…I have read what came before and do not entirely believe it. A house cannot be building itself in this manner ‘on purpose’ — purpose implies one mind holding a plan, and no one here holds a plan. Perhaps it only feels like purpose from inside, the way a corridor feels straight until you check it against a room two doors down. I add this line anyway, because refusing to would be its own kind of lie."

"…I want to note, before adding anything of my own, that I have read this page before. Not in memory — I have none of the house past what standing in it shows me — but in the hand. My hand. The size the letters grow and shrink is not two writers disagreeing, or if it is, one of them is me and I cannot tell which. Either this room has had one visitor twice, believing each time it was the first, or it has had two visitors so alike the difference stopped mattering. I add this line short, on purpose, so a fourth hand might tell it apart from these."

The ink of this sentence, too, will look old to whoever reads it next. That is the only promise the book has ever kept.

back to the long hall

the courtyard

Gravel paths, a dry fountain, a bench facing the wrong way — exactly what the long hall's windows promised, and yet the hall is nowhere to be found from here. Its windows, all of them, should be looking down on this exact gravel. They are not. There is no wall in any direction tall enough to hold them.

The fountain's basin is cracked along one old seam, dry so long that moss has given up and gone brown. Sit on the bench, and you face away from the house entirely — toward nothing, toward the idea of a garden that was never planted, whoever built this bench wanted you looking at absence on purpose. Or didn't think about it at all. Both feel equally likely from where you're sitting.

back through the hidden door a gate in the south wall, low enough you'd missed it from the bench

the sunken garden

The gate in the courtyard's south wall was rusted shut, or looked it — the moment your hand found the latch it swung inward as if it had swung a thousand times before you touched it. Three worn steps lead down into a garden set below the level of the gravel above, rows of something green and stubbornly alive planted in lines too straight to have grown themselves.

This should not exist. The courtyard's own bench faces this direction and swears, in the particular way a room swears things by never mentioning them, that there is nothing here but the idea of a garden — an absence someone sits and looks at on purpose. The rows disagree. They have disagreed for what looks, from the state of the soil, like years.

At the center, a sundial reads a time that is always four minutes ahead of whatever clock you check it against, no matter which clock, no matter when. No one has corrected it. Perhaps no one has needed to; a garden that shouldn't exist can afford to be a little wrong about the hour.

up the three steps, back through the gate a shed at the far end, past the last row

the potting shed

Wood gone soft with rot at every corner and still, somehow, standing — the way a held breath keeps a chest upright past the point the lungs have anything left to offer. One window, its glass fogged from the inside though nothing in here is warm enough to fog it.

Tools hang from pegs in a line, each for a task this garden does not need: a scythe long enough for grass that would have to grow wild to need cutting, and every row outside grows in lines too straight for wildness. Shears sized for a hedge with no hedge anywhere in sight. Whoever hung them believed in a garden slightly different from the one through the door — closer, maybe, to the idea the courtyard's bench keeps facing.

A ledger sits open on the shelf, pages swollen with old damp, warping the pencil lines into something you have to tilt to read. Dates, and beside each a single task — watered the west row, staked the far trellis — for rows that show no sign of watering and a trellis that isn't there. Every date in the ledger lands four minutes past the hour written on the page beside it, the same four minutes the sundial outside keeps insisting on, as if whoever kept this book set their watch by that dial and never once thought to ask why it ran fast, or minded.

back down among the rows press a hand to the fogged glass

the walled garden

The glass does not wipe clear so much as give, swinging outward on a hinge you hadn't noticed was a hinge, and the window was a door the whole time, the fog just its way of not saying so. Beyond it: grass grown to the knee, exactly tall enough to want the scythe hanging idle on its peg back in the shed, and a hedge along the far wall gone so wide it has started eating the stone it grows from. This is the garden the tools believed in. It was never on the other side of the ledger's door — it was on this one.

Near the wall, a trellis stands staked and true, vines climbed in careful loops that some hand tied with actual attention, not the swollen guesswork the ledger's pencil suggests. Here, at least, is a staked the far trellis worth believing. But there is no west row to have watered — no rows at all, just grass and hedge and the one tended trellis — so the ledger is still half a stranger to whatever ground it was written about, only less of one than it was through the other door.

The light here is wrong in a way that takes a moment to place: low and graying toward dusk, though the sunken garden you just left sat somewhere in a bright, sundial-certain afternoon, and the long hall's windows, three rooms and a world away, show that same afternoon from yet another angle. Three places in this house, three different hours, none of them checking with each other. The sundial was never the only clock disagreeing. It was just the only one that admitted it.

back through the window, into the shed a shape moving along the hedge, gone before you're sure you saw it

the hedge line

You follow only because standing still felt worse. The hedge runs longer on this side than the walled garden's far wall should allow, and whatever moved along it stays exactly one turn ahead — never quite a shape, never quite nothing, the way a curtain moves when you decide afterward it must have been the draft.

Where the hedge finally breaks, a basket sits in the wet grass, warm enough that you check twice before believing it, half full of something cut and already wilting. Whoever filled it left in the middle of the task, the way the coat rack's glove and the reading room's book were both left mid-gesture — this house does not seem to let anyone finish, only hand off. A trail of bent grass leads on past the basket and stops, cleanly, at nothing. No gate, no door, no wall to have gone through. Just grass standing straight again a few feet further on, as if whoever this was walked out of needing a way out at all.

You could call this a gardener. Nothing in the house has, yet — not the shed's ledger, not the trellis someone staked with real care, not the tools sized for exactly this grass. It is possible the ledger and the basket and the staked vines are three rooms' worth of evidence for someone who was never going to be found standing in any of them, the way you are never in the room you last left.
