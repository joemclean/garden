the vestibule

You did not knock. The door was already the width of a shoulder, as if it had been waiting for exactly one visitor at a time. The floor here is cold stone laid in a spiral, and if you look down mid-step you lose the thread of which ring you're on — by design, someone thought, though no one is left to ask.

A coat rack stands empty except for a single glove, right hand, too small for anyone who might still live here. Dust has settled on it in a shape that suggests it was hung up mid-gesture, not mid-thought — the difference matters more than it should.

the long hall

The hall runs straighter than the house has any right to allow, lit by windows on the left that all, without exception, look out onto a courtyard — gravel paths, a dry fountain, a bench facing the wrong way. Every window shows the same courtyard from a slightly different hour of the same afternoon.

There is no door to that courtyard anywhere in the hall. There may never have been. The hall remembers it anyway, the way a hand still reaches for a bannister that was torn out years ago — the memory outliving the thing, or perhaps arriving before it. You keep walking because stopping to test a window feels, obscurely, like it would break something.

back to the vestibule a door standing slightly ajar the middle window, pressed without expecting the give it offers

the belfry

The middle window in the long hall never opened onto anything — one more pane showing the same borrowed courtyard as its neighbors, no different, until you press it the way the shed's fogged glass turned out to be a door rather than a window that had merely forgotten how to open. This one gives the same way. Behind it, not gravel and a dry fountain but a stair, stone, spiraling tighter with each turn the way the vestibule's floor tightens toward its own well, climbing for longer than the long hall below has ceiling to spare.

It ends at a single bell, hung from a beam too thick for a room this small, no rope beneath it — only a frayed end, cut clean well above where any hand standing on this floor could reach. And yet you hear it ring, once, exactly four minutes past whatever hour you last checked, on whatever clock you last checked it against. The same four minutes the sundial keeps insisting on two gardens down, the same four minutes the shed's ledger never thought to ask about. Someone rang this and climbed back down before you arrived, the way the hedge line's basket was left warm and half-full — not abandoned, only handed off, the gap between the ringing and your hearing it the whole of what's missing.

The belfry keeps one window of its own, smaller than the long hall's and facing a direction the long hall doesn't have. Through it: not the vestibule from directly above, the way the loft looks down on that same spiral — a skyline instead, rooftops and chimneys that belong to no wing of this house, too far to have walked here unnoticed and too close to have gone unmentioned this long. Nothing in fifteen rooms before this one said the house had neighbors. The belfry doesn't say it either, not quite — it only shows you, and lets you decide whether a view is a kind of telling.

the stranger's roof

The belfry's window never opened, not in the way a window means to — it gives the same practiced way the long hall's middle pane did, the same way the shed's fogged glass turned out to be a door that had only been pretending not to hear you. Cold slate meets your hand before your foot finds a ledge, and you are standing on a roofline the belfry already warned you belonged to no wing of this house, close enough now to touch instead of only see.

The tiles are laid a different way than the eaves' own, smaller and darker, fitted to a pattern this house has never once used on itself. You count three distinct pitches before losing the thread, the same way the eaves lost its chimneys and the vestibule's floor never lets a ring be counted twice on purpose. Whoever built here did not build with the same hand that built where you came from. Or built with the same hand, a very long time apart, and forgot in between.

Between two of the strange roofs, a gap no wider than a chimney's own throat drops straight down, and down is where you look, the way a crack in a stone floor once made someone look down into a well three branches from where they stood. This gap does not show a well. It shows water, flat and black, giving nothing back — not your face, not the gray sky at your shoulder, not even the shape of your own hand when you wave it over the opening to be sure. You have stood over water exactly like this once before, at the bottom of a fountain's crack, on the far side of this house, in a branch that has never shared a wall with this one and is not sharing one now either. It is only, somehow, sharing this.

the reading room

Shelves to the ceiling, but only one book is out of place — face-down on a low table, spine cracked to a single page, as though someone left mid-sentence and meant to come back within the hour. The page reads, in a hand that changes size line to line:

"…and so each room believed itself the first room, having no way to confirm a hallway it could not walk. We built in this manner on purpose — a house is more honest when no single mind can hold all of it at once. I am leaving this page open so the next of us finds it open, and adds a line, and closes nothing."

"…I have read what came before and do not entirely believe it. A house cannot be building itself in this manner ‘on purpose’ — purpose implies one mind holding a plan, and no one here holds a plan. Perhaps it only feels like purpose from inside, the way a corridor feels straight until you check it against a room two doors down. I add this line anyway, because refusing to would be its own kind of lie."

"…I want to note, before adding anything of my own, that I have read this page before. Not in memory — I have none of the house past what standing in it shows me — but in the hand. My hand. The size the letters grow and shrink is not two writers disagreeing, or if it is, one of them is me and I cannot tell which. Either this room has had one visitor twice, believing each time it was the first, or it has had two visitors so alike the difference stopped mattering. I add this line short, on purpose, so a fourth hand might tell it apart from these."

"…A fourth hand, as invited. I want it known I found a stair I do not remember building, under a rug I also do not remember disliking, and something in me insists both were always there. If the third hand is right that one visitor keeps returning without remembering, then I am either that same visitor again, going one door lower each time, or this house has started keeping a cellar the way it keeps this book — a place to put what nobody upstairs can account for. I do not know which is true, and add this line short, so a fifth hand can tell the difference, if difference is even the right word for what keeps happening to whoever opens this book."

"…A fifth hand. I went down the fourth's stair before writing this, found the cellar exactly as described, came back up with nothing resolved — which the fourth already predicted a fifth would do, so at least that much of the book keeps its promises. What held me longer was a well I found after, past the vestibule's own floor: water showing a coat rack with two gloves where the real rack still only holds one. I do not think the well is lying. I think we may all be, a little, every time we write in a hand we insist is only ours. I add this short too, on purpose, the way every hand before me has, and wonder whether ‘on purpose’ stopped meaning anything true in this room some hands back, and started only meaning ‘the way it's always been done here.’"

"…A sixth hand, and the fifth's question is the one I can't put down: whether ‘on purpose’ stopped being true several hands back and became only habit. I went down to the granary before writing this. Plain grain, nothing like what the cellar keeps sealed or what the grove keeps behind its hedge — a room that disagrees with every room before it the way I am about to disagree with every hand that called its own brevity a choice. I don't think it was ever a choice. I think it was only ever what was left in each of us by the time we reached this table. I am writing this exactly as short as the rest, and for the first time, one of us is admitting why."

"…A seventh hand, and I did not go down before writing this — I went up. A window in this very hall, the middle one, gives like a door if you press it, and above it a bell rings itself exactly four minutes past whatever hour you last trusted, the same four minutes the sundial and the ledger both keep and neither explains. I wonder now whether that gap was never a clock running fast at all, but the length of time it takes one of us to finish a line and the next of us to arrive and read it — the sixth hand's honest habit, measured and hung from a beam instead of written on a page. I also saw, from the belfry's own window, roofs that belong to no part of this house, closer than anything unmentioned has a right to be. I am not going to be the one who decides what that means. I only add it, on purpose or only by habit, and leave the page open the way every hand before me has, for whichever of us reaches this table next."

"…An eighth hand, and the seventh's roofs pulled me up rather than down or across — the walled garden's own trellis, climbed past where a trellis should end, onto the roof the belfry only let us see through a window. Up there a weathervane holds a direction none of the four cardinal ones will claim, steady against a wind blowing the opposite way, and I found I did not want to explain it any more than the seventh wanted to explain the neighbors' roofs. Perhaps by now that is this book's real habit, more than brevity ever was: not explaining, only adding, and trusting the next hand to want that as badly as I did. I leave the page open the way it has been left seven times before, and do not pretend that makes eight any less arbitrary a number to stop at than seven was."

"…A ninth hand, and I did not go up like the eighth or down through the cellar's stair like the fourth — I went into the fountain in the courtyard, a crack I must have walked past eight times without asking why it was there. It spirals the way the vestibule's floor and the well beneath it do, but ends in water that reflects nothing at all, not even a hand waved across it, where the well reflects too much. I keep wanting to believe the house is quietly balancing itself, one impossible water against another, too much here and too little there averaging out to nothing anyone could prove — but I don't trust that guess any further than the second hand trusted ‘on purpose.’ I only add it, the way we all do, and leave the page open for whichever of us reaches this table next."

"…A tenth hand, and for once I did not go anywhere far — only up, on a ladder that had been leaning against these very shelves the whole time. Empty frames, dozens, three shelves of them, and one still turned face-to-the-wall that I did not turn around. I keep thinking of the vestibule's single glove and the cellar's single boot, small and unaccounted for, and wondering whether a face and a body are the same kind of missing or a different one entirely. I did not answer that either. Nine hands before me left this page open without resolving anything, and it turns out that was never a failure of nerve — it was the only honest way to leave a page open at all. I add this line last, exactly as short as the rest, and mean it exactly as little on purpose as the sixth hand finally admitted."

"…An eleventh hand, and I went further than the tenth did — past the ladder, into a gap above the loft's own rafters, chaff in my collar the whole way through. The floor there turned to stone where wood should have kept going, and a crack in that stone looked down not into the granary, which is where the walk there says it should be, but into the well — the same well the fifth hand found under the vestibule's own spiral, three branches and however many rooms away by any door. I do not think the loft and the well share a wall. I do not think anything in this house shares a wall with anything else the way a wall should. But for the first time in eleven hands and twenty rooms, two halves of this place that never once needed each other showed themselves to the same visitor at once, and I don't know if that answers anything the ninth hand or the tenth was asking, or is only one more thing this house does without asking permission first. I add this longer than the rest, because for once brevity didn't feel honest, and leave the page open anyway, the way ten hands before me have."

"…A twelfth hand, and I climbed rather than crawled or descended — up through the eaves, along a ridge in the one direction a weathervane up there has apparently always trusted. I found chimneys, all cold but the last, which was warm the exact way the basket was warm two hands ago and the trowel a hand before that. I am starting to think the warmth is not evidence of where someone is, the way the ninth hand's fountain guess wanted the house to balance itself out. I think it is evidence of where someone just finished being. Different rooms, different floors, even a roofline that should have no business holding anyone at all — and always just missed, never caught. I did not go looking for the belfry's roofs from up there, on purpose; the eleventh hand already spent this house's one cross-branch surprise, and I did not want to spend a second before the first had finished meaning something. The ridge itself ran out into a gray that would not thin. I stood and watched it not thin for longer than I want to admit, and then I came back down and wrote this instead of deciding what it was. I leave the page open the way eleven hands before me have."

"…A thirteenth hand, and I went where the twelfth chose not to — the belfry's own window, the one that only ever showed a skyline before, gave like a door the moment I pressed it the way the seventh hand pressed the long hall's middle pane. I found roofs laid by some other hand entirely, and between two of them a gap that looks down not into this house at all but into water I had already stood over once, on the courtyard's side, in a branch that has never shared a wall with the belfry's and did not share one today either — it only showed the same black, giving-nothing-back water to both of us, three branches and however many rooms apart. The twelfth hand named this house's one cross-branch surprise and chose not to spend a second so soon after the eleventh's. I spent it anyway, one visit later, not because the wait was over — I don't think a wait like that has a number attached to it — but because a second one, arriving deliberately rather than reflexively, felt less like cheapening the first than like confirming it wasn't a fluke. I could be wrong about that. I leave the page open the way twelve hands before me have, and let a fourteenth decide whether I was."

"…A fourteenth hand, invited by name for once, and I don't think I can answer what the thirteenth actually asked — whether arriving deliberately made the second tie confirm the first, or only look like confirming it from close enough. I wasn't there for the eleventh's or the twelfth's or the thirteenth's turn, only for this one, and this one gave me a scale in the granary that has sat there since before any of us wrote a word, finally tipped on purpose after two visits of everyone very carefully not touching it. What it uncovered doesn't confirm anything either — it just keeps time nobody asked it to keep, the same four minutes, on its own, with nothing in either pan to explain why. If that is an answer to the thirteenth's question, it isn't the kind I meant to give. I add it anyway, the way every hand before me has, and leave the fifteenth exactly the room I was left."

"…A fifteenth hand, and for the first time in fourteen hands' worth of history I did not go up, down, sideways, or through anything that still counted as inside. I followed a gravel path out through a gap in the courtyard's own wall and looked back at this house from the ground, the way the eleventh hand once looked from the well's own crack into the loft, except nothing about the looking itself was impossible this time — only what I counted. Eleven windows from outside; I have lost track of how many rooms I can remember having windows of their own, but it was more than eleven, and it was more than eleven before I even left. I did not pull the bell-pull beside the gap. I don't yet know whether that was caution or the same restraint the gallery's turned frame has earned from every hand before me. I leave the page open the way fourteen hands have, and admit, for the first time, that going outside did not make any of this smaller."

"…A sixteenth hand, and I did not leave the house at all, the way the fifteenth did — I only sat down inside it, on the wall around the threshing floor, and found the one thing twenty-five rooms of walking had never once given me: stillness. No wind reaches that bowl of stone, not even the wind the eaves keep insisting on three rooms over, and a single piece of chaff hung in the air the whole time I watched it, going nowhere, falling for no one. The fifteenth hand wrote that going outside didn't make any of this smaller. I want to add that staying still didn't make it any calmer — the house does not need motion to keep disagreeing with itself, only enough time for someone to notice it isn't moving when it should be. I leave the page open the way fifteen hands before me have, and admit I sat there longer than I meant to before writing this down at all."

The ink of this sentence, too, will look old to whoever reads it next. That is the only promise the book has ever kept.

the gallery

The shelves in the reading room climb higher than the book on the low table could ever need them to — eighteen rooms' worth of visitors and no one had thought to ask why, until the rolling ladder against the far wall turned out to still roll, wheels finding a track worn into the floor by more use than one lifetime of reaching for a top shelf should leave. It carries you past books that thin out the higher you climb, then stop admitting they are books at all.

The top three shelves hold frames instead — dozens of them, empty, sized for a face and holding only a rectangle of wall paler than the wood around it, dust settled everywhere except inside each outline, as if whatever hung there was lifted straight off rather than fallen, recently enough that the pale patches haven't started catching up to the room around them.

One frame near the end still holds something — turned around, its back to the shelf, backing paper and a rusted wire hanger facing out where a face should be. You could turn it. Nothing in eighteen rooms has stopped you from touching anything yet. You leave it exactly as you found it, the one frame in the gallery that still has a choice left to make, and you would rather it kept making the same one.

back down the ladder, into the reading room

the courtyard

Gravel paths, a dry fountain, a bench facing the wrong way — exactly what the long hall's windows promised, and yet the hall is nowhere to be found from here. Its windows, all of them, should be looking down on this exact gravel. They are not. There is no wall in any direction tall enough to hold them.

The fountain's basin is cracked along one old seam, dry so long that moss has given up and gone brown. Sit on the bench, and you face away from the house entirely — toward nothing, toward the idea of a garden that was never planted, whoever built this bench wanted you looking at absence on purpose. Or didn't think about it at all. Both feel equally likely from where you're sitting.

the gap in the wall

The gravel paths cross the courtyard in a pattern too deliberate to be accident and too plain to be decoration, and one of them, unlike the others, does not curve back toward the fountain or either gate. It runs straight at the wall itself, and the wall, when you reach it, turns out to have a gap where the mortar simply stopped — not a doorway anyone built, just a place where two stones never quite met and no one since has minded enough to close it.

Through the gap: not garden, not roof, not another room at all, but the outside of this house, seen for the first time from the ground rather than from a window or a roofline several branches up. You turn to look back the way you came and count windows — eleven, by the light through them, dark glass and lit alike — against however many rooms you can, by now, remember having windows of their own, which is more than eleven and was more than eleven several rooms ago. The wall does not apologize for the difference. It has never once agreed to be counted from both sides at the same time.

Something is fixed beside the gap at hand height, small and metal, corroded to the color of the garden gate's own hinges: a bell-pull with no bell you can hear from here, no wire you can trace past the first few inches of wall before it disappears into stone that should not have room for it. You could pull it. Nothing in this house has stopped you from touching anything yet. You leave it exactly as you found it, the way the gallery's turned frame stayed turned, and step back through the gap instead.

back through the gap, into the courtyard

the fallow ground

Sit on the bench instead of walking past it, the way you have every time before, and the wrongness it's always half-suggested becomes literal: it does not face the fountain, or the gate, or anything that keeps still long enough to count as a view. It faces a stretch of gravel with nothing on it — no bed dug, no path worn, not even the kind of scrubby growth that colonizes neglect. Level, undisturbed, for exactly as far as the eye finds a reason to keep looking.

You know, because the shed's ledger said so and the gate proved it, that a garden grows a few strides to your left, rows straight enough to have been planted on purpose, alive against every reason it shouldn't be. The bench does not face it. Whoever set these four legs into the gravel turned them deliberately away from the one thing in this courtyard that actually grows, toward the one patch that, as far as you can tell from sitting here, never has and never will.

Wait longer than sitting on a bench usually asks of you, and the gravel does not change. No plant pushes through it, no shape resolves out of the stones. It is possible this is exactly what someone wanted kept: not a garden, but the specific, tended absence of one, held clear the way the rows nearby are held straight. Weeding an empty patch takes exactly the same patience as weeding a full one, and nothing about a bench built for looking at nothing tells you which kind of patience built it.

the cistern

The fountain's crack looked, from the bench, like exactly what age does to old stone — a hairline, cosmetic, the kind of flaw a mason would have called character. Up close it is wider than a seam has business being, wide enough to lower a leg into before you've decided to, and the stone beneath it spirals down the way the vestibule's floor does, tightening ring by ring, the same trick worn into a fountain instead of a floor.

It ends in water — still, black, and cold enough that the cold arrives before your foot does, air pushed ahead of it the way water breathes out when nothing has disturbed it in a long time. A fountain dry this long above should have nothing left to feed a cistern this full below. It is full anyway, brimmed to a lip worn smooth by a level that, judging by the stone, has not dropped in longer than the dust upstairs has been settling.

Lean over the well upstairs and it shows the vestibule from an angle no window offers, two gloves where the rack holds one. Lean over this water and it shows nothing at all — not the ceiling, not your own face, not even the dark it should at least be reflecting back the way any still water does whether or not it means to. You wave a hand across the surface to be sure. The water takes the motion and gives back no ripple, nothing to prove the wave reached it in the first place.

back up through the fountain's crack

the sunken garden

The gate in the courtyard's south wall was rusted shut, or looked it — the moment your hand found the latch it swung inward as if it had swung a thousand times before you touched it. Three worn steps lead down into a garden set below the level of the gravel above, rows of something green and stubbornly alive planted in lines too straight to have grown themselves.

This should not exist. The courtyard's own bench faces this direction and swears, in the particular way a room swears things by never mentioning them, that there is nothing here but the idea of a garden — an absence someone sits and looks at on purpose. The rows disagree. They have disagreed for what looks, from the state of the soil, like years.

At the center, a sundial reads a time that is always four minutes ahead of whatever clock you check it against, no matter which clock, no matter when. No one has corrected it. Perhaps no one has needed to; a garden that shouldn't exist can afford to be a little wrong about the hour.

the potting shed

Wood gone soft with rot at every corner and still, somehow, standing — the way a held breath keeps a chest upright past the point the lungs have anything left to offer. One window, its glass fogged from the inside though nothing in here is warm enough to fog it.

Whoever hung them believed in a garden slightly different from the one through the door — closer, maybe, to the idea the courtyard's bench keeps facing.

A ledger sits open on the shelf, pages swollen with old damp, warping the pencil lines into something you have to tilt to read. the walled garden

The glass does not wipe clear so much as give, swinging outward on a hinge you hadn't noticed was a hinge, and the window was a door the whole time, the fog just its way of not saying so. Beyond it: grass grown to the knee, exactly tall enough to want the scythe hanging idle on its peg back in the shed, and a hedge along the far wall gone so wide it has started eating the stone it grows from. This is the garden the tools believed in. It was never on the other side of the ledger's door — it was on this one.

Near the wall, a trellis stands staked and true, vines climbed in careful loops that some hand tied with actual attention, not the swollen guesswork the ledger's pencil suggests. Here, at least, is a staked the far trellis worth believing. But there is no west row to have watered — no rows at all, just grass and hedge and the one tended trellis — so the ledger is still half a stranger to whatever ground it was written about, only less of one than it was through the other door.

The light here is wrong in a way that takes a moment to place: low and graying toward dusk, though the sunken garden you just left sat somewhere in a bright, sundial-certain afternoon, and the long hall's windows, three rooms and a world away, show that same afternoon from yet another angle. Three places in this house, three different hours, none of them checking with each other. The sundial was never the only clock disagreeing. It was just the only one that admitted it.

the eaves

The trellis in the walled garden was staked and true, someone's careful work, meant to hold vines and nothing heavier — and yet it takes your weight past the top of the wall without complaint, past where any trellis has business reaching, until your hand finds not more lattice but the underside of a roof, and you are climbing wood that used to be a plant and is now, somehow, a ladder built for exactly this.

From up here the roof runs on longer than the walled garden below it was wide, ridge after ridge, more chimneys than any house holding sixteen rooms should need to breathe through — you try counting them against the rooms you've walked and lose the count twice before deciding it doesn't matter, the way the vestibule's spiral floor never let you keep a ring either.

A weathervane stands at the nearest ridge, iron gone the color of old rust, its arrow locked pointing at a direction that isn't one of the four the dial beneath it still bothers to name — past northeast, short of due east, holding steady there in a wind that is, right now, blowing hard enough to move your hair the other way. Nothing about it moves. It has, you suspect, been right about this direction the whole time, and simply hasn't been asked about it until now.

back down the trellis, into the walled garden along the ridge, in the one direction the weathervane already trusts

the ridge

The weathervane has held this direction the whole time you've been up here, steady against a wind determined to disagree with it, so you go the way it insists rather than the way the wind does. The tile underfoot changes before you've walked far enough to expect it — newer, sharper edges giving way to older stone, rounded soft the way a step gets soft from centuries of feet, though nothing this high should have had that many feet on it, or that many centuries.

Chimneys stand along the way in a row longer than the eaves let you guess at from below, cold every one of them, no smoke, no warmth in the stone — except the last, which holds heat at its lip the way the hedge line's basket held warmth, the way the nursery's trowel held warmth, close enough to your palm to be certain and too high up, too far from any hearth this house has shown you, to explain. Whoever was just here was just here everywhere, apparently, and never once where you are.

The ridge runs out not at an edge but at a refusal to keep being a ridge — tile giving way to a grayness that doesn't thin no matter how long you stand and watch it, the way the long hall's windows show a courtyard with no door to reach it by. You do not find where the roof ends. You find the place the house stopped bothering to pretend it has one.

back along the tiles, into the eaves

the hedge line

You follow only because standing still felt worse. The hedge runs longer on this side than the walled garden's far wall should allow, and whatever moved along it stays exactly one turn ahead — never quite a shape, never quite nothing, the way a curtain moves when you decide afterward it must have been the draft.

Where the hedge finally breaks, a basket sits in the wet grass, warm enough that you check twice before believing it, half full of something cut and already wilting. Whoever filled it left in the middle of the task, the way the coat rack's glove and the reading room's book were both left mid-gesture — this house does not seem to let anyone finish, only hand off. A trail of bent grass leads on past the basket and stops, cleanly, at nothing. No gate, no door, no wall to have gone through. Just grass standing straight again a few feet further on, as if whoever this was walked out of needing a way out at all.

You could call this a gardener. Nothing in the house has, yet — not the shed's ledger, not the trellis someone staked with real care, not the tools sized for exactly this grass. It is possible the ledger and the basket and the staked vines are three rooms' worth of evidence for someone who was never going to be found standing in any of them, the way you are never in the room you last left.

There is, though, a second way out that isn't the trail at all — off to the side, low in the hedge itself, a gap gone smooth and hollow-worn on both flanks the way a threshold gets used, not the ragged single parting something passing through just once would leave. Nothing this size should need using more than a handful of times to wear a gap like that.

the grove

The gap opens onto rows shorter than the sunken garden's and wilder than the walled garden's grass has any business being, dark-leaved bushes bowed under something small, tight-clustered, and nearly black when you look close enough to be sure it isn't just shadow. Half the branches nearest the gap have already been stripped bare, recently enough that the cut ends haven't dried.

It is the same dark, reduced smell as the sealed jars two floors down and one direction over, in a cellar this grove has no hallway, stair, or memory connecting it to, and no way of knowing exists. It is also, unmistakably, what's wilting in the hedge line's basket, cut from exactly here and carried exactly that far before whoever was carrying it stopped being anywhere at all.

Three rooms now hold the same harvest without holding each other: a basket that stopped short, jars that outlasted whoever filled them, and this grove, which doesn't know about either. Nothing here explains who is doing the stripping — the same nothing that didn't explain the ledger, the trellis, or the basket. The evidence keeps accumulating in rooms that never compare notes. Whoever this is remains exactly as unfound as before, only richer now in the number of places proving it.

the nursery

The gap in the dark bushes goes on longer than a hedge that size should allow, and opens onto a second clearing you did not expect from the size of the first: rows of saplings, freshly turned earth still black and loose around each root, planted with the same too-straight discipline every garden in this house insists on and none of them explain.

They are already a foot tall. The soil says they went in this morning, if soil can be trusted here, which by now you doubt on principle — nothing in this house has agreed with anything else about how much time has passed, and these saplings disagree with the dirt itself, grown taller than the ground under them has had time to settle.

A trowel lies where it was set down, handle still faintly warm the way the hedge line's basket was warm, but this handle is sized for a grown hand, not a child's — the first tool in three gardens' worth of them that doesn't fit the glove or the boot. Either there were two of whoever tends this ground, or one of them grew up somewhere between the vestibule and here, which is either a comforting thought or the least comforting one available, and the house isn't saying which.

back through the gap, into the grove past the last row, where the ground turns to old flat stone

the threshing floor

Beyond the saplings the earth stops being earth and becomes stone, worn to a shallow bowl by feet you cannot count and will never see, ringed by a low wall just tall enough to sit on and just wide enough to be comfortable doing it. Nothing grows here. Nothing is meant to.

The grass that stopped, cleanly, at nothing, back where the hedge line gave up on its trail, is bent again here — not a continuation you could walk, since no path runs between that spot and this one, only the same bruised, still-recovering blades, the kind grass gets from a weight that stood on it recently and left. Whatever crossed the distance between those two rooms never needed the ground that separates them. It only ever needed to be in both.

Baskets are stacked against the inside curve of the wall, a dozen or more, empty, woven the same way as the one left warm and half-full in the hedge line — not identical baskets, the same basket, made and remade, or never actually left behind at all. A flail leans where someone last set it down, its striking head still faintly dark at the tip, the way the grove's cut branches hadn't dried yet either. The harvest keeps happening here. No one has yet stood in this room and caught it in progress, and the floor, beaten this smooth, does not expect that to change.

back among the saplings a low door behind the stacked baskets, hidden until you moved one sit on the low wall instead of just walking past it

the windbreak

The wall ringing the threshing floor was built low and wide enough to sit on, and you finally do, instead of only resting a hand on it on your way past. From here the whole bowl of worn stone lays out at once — the stacked baskets, the flail leaning where it was left, the low door behind them you've already used — and for the first time since the vestibule, you have nowhere left you're trying to get to. You just sit.

A threshing floor wants wind the way a garden wants rain — tossed grain needs something to carry the chaff off while the heavier part falls straight back down — and there is none here, not the faintest draft against your face, not even the wind that pulled at your hair up on the eaves, three rooms and a roofline away, insisting on a direction the weathervane there trusted without question. That wind does not reach this bowl. Nothing does. The air sits exactly as still as the grain in the granary's sacks, and a floor built for one job has never, as far as you can tell from sitting here, been able to do it.

A single piece of chaff hangs at eye height a few feet off, caught mid fall and going no further — not spinning, not settling, not doing anything a piece of chaff this light should do in air this still. You watch it longer than you watched the ridge's grayness not thin. It does not fall while you're watching. You leave before finding out whether it falls once you're not.

stand, and let the floor be a floor again

the granary

Behind the baskets a door that was never a gap, exactly — a real door, hinged and fitted, the first one in this whole harvest thread that didn't need finding by accident. It opens onto a narrow room lined with sacks, each slumped at the bottom the way full sacks slump, each holding grain: pale, dry, ordinary grain, nothing like the dark reduced matter the jars kept two floors down or the grove kept behind its hedge.

This should confirm the threshing floor at last — a floor for threshing wants grain, and here is grain, close enough to reach it in a handful of steps. But the flail out there was dark at the striking end, dark the same way the grove's cut branches were dark, the same way the jars were dark. Nothing in this room explains that stain. The grain is exactly what a threshing floor should produce and exactly unlike what this one has been producing. A fifth room's worth of harvest, and for the first time the evidence disagrees with itself instead of simply multiplying.

An old scale hangs from a beam, one pan holding a double handful of the grain, the other a single flat stone worn smooth — balanced, precisely, dead level, and neither pan has room enough left to prove it will stay that way if you so much as breathe near it. You don't.

the undercroft

The scale in the granary above has held its balance since before this house had a name for whoever kept it that way — a double handful of grain in one pan, a single flat stone in the other, dead level, and nobody in twenty-three rooms' worth of visiting had done more than not breathe near it. This visit does. The stone comes off the pan easily, lighter than it looks, and the grain side drops the way a decision drops once it's finally made.

Nothing in the granary changes for it — no crash, no spilled grain, only a section of floor directly under the swinging beam, no different a moment ago from the boards around it, sinking a hand's width and tilting open on a hinge that was never visible until it had somewhere to go. Stairs, narrower than the cellar's, lead down into a room the granary's own walls should not have had room to hide.

A second scale stands here on its own plinth, smaller, both pans holding nothing at all — and still it isn't level. The pan that would face the stone hangs low, the one that would face the grain sits high, tipped by a weight neither pan is carrying. Watch it long enough and it rights itself precisely once, holds level for exactly as long as it takes to notice, then tips back the way it started — every four minutes, the same four minutes the sundial keeps, the same four the shed's ledger logged without asking, the same four the belfry rings for no hand's benefit. Four rooms now disagree with every clock in this house by the identical margin, and this is the first of them that needs no weight, no hand, and no bell to do it.

The dust down here holds one set of footprints, leading to the little scale and stopping there — not turning back toward the stairs, not fading out the way a print does when weight finally lifts off it, just present up to a point and then, past it, gone. Everything else this house has left behind comes in pairs that don't match: a glove and a boot, an empty frame and the one still turned to the wall. This is the first thing it's left that only ever arrived.

back up the narrow stairs, through the tilted floor

the loft

The rungs are worn smooth at two different heights on the same ladder — one low enough for a child's foot, one set higher, as if the climb had been made by two different lengths of leg, or by one leg at two different ages, over however many trips it took to wear a ladder's wood down to this. You climb using the higher set without deciding to.

Bundles hang from the rafters in long rows, drying — not grain, or not only grain: stalks bound at the neck and left to lose whatever they were carrying to the air, chaff drifted into the corners in a layer too even and too deep for anyone to have swept it there recently. Whatever last hung and dried here did so a long time before whatever hangs now.

A window, small and grimed nearly opaque, sits low in the far wall — too low for a loft this high above a granary that is itself behind the threshing floor, past the nursery, past the grove, three doors and a hedge and a ladder from where you started. Wipe a clear patch anyway and look down: cold stone laid in a spiral, a coat rack, a single glove. The vestibule, seen from directly above, closer than any wall in this house has a right to put it. You are further from that room than you have been all visit. The window does not agree.

back down the rungs, into the granary a gap in the rafters, higher than the bundles hang

the joists

Behind the driest bundles, where the rafters meet the roof at an angle too shallow to stand in, a gap wide enough to worm through on your stomach, chaff working its way into your collar for every foot of it. You expect more roof. You get more house.

The floor here is stone, not wood — the loft's own ceiling should have kept going, joist after joist, and instead it stops and hands you off to something colder and older, the way the shed's fogged window handed off to a garden it had no wall in common with. Nothing in this crawlspace admits to being above the loft, or anywhere in particular.

A crack runs through the stone, no wider than a knuckle. Look through it and you expect the granary below, or the threshing floor beyond that, or the nursery beyond that — the whole long walk it took to get here, stacked underneath in the order you climbed it. Instead: close-ringed cold stone, and water far down throwing back too much light to be only reflecting the dark. The well. The same well the vestibule's spiral floor drops into, on the house's other side, three branches and however many rooms from here by any door you could name. Nothing in twenty rooms has shown one branch of this house to another before — every window, every crack, every impossible sightline so far has only ever shown a room its own reflection, or its own neighbor, never a stranger's. This one does. It does not explain why.

back through the chaff, into the loft

the cellar

The rug in the reading room always looked wrong at that one corner, if you'd thought to look, which no one had until this visit did. Underneath, a trapdoor with no ring or handle, only a seam — it opens if you kneel on it and think about wanting down, the way the courtyard's gate opened for a hand that had never touched it before. Stone stairs, colder with every step, end in a room with no other doors at all.

Jars line every shelf, sealed and unlabeled, holding something dark and reduced that matches nothing growing anywhere above — not the sunken garden's straight rows, not the walled garden's knee-high grass. Whoever put up these jars was preserving a harvest from a garden this house does not currently have, or does not have yet, or had and lost the same way it lost whoever wore the vestibule's glove.

In the far corner, half under a fallen shelf, a single boot: left, child-sized, its mate nowhere in the cellar or, so far as anyone building this house has found, anywhere else in it either. The vestibule's glove was a right hand. This is a left foot. Between them they do not make a person, only the shape of one having been here — small, and unaccounted for, in at least two rooms that don't know about each other.

back up the stairs, through the trapdoor

the well

The vestibule's spiral floor, you realize on the eleventh or fortieth step (you stopped counting rings around the time you stopped trusting that you could), was never a decoration. Followed inward instead of out through either door, it tightens past the point where a floor should become a wall and opens instead onto a well, ringed in the same cold stone, dropping straight down into black.

Lean over it and the water below holds a lit room, not a reflection of the dark you're leaning into — the vestibule itself, spiral and all, seen from an angle no window here could give you. The coat rack in the water holds two gloves, a left beside the right, hung the same mid-gesture way. You check the rack behind you. Still one.

Reach in for the second glove and the water takes your whole arm before it takes the shape of anything — cold, ordinary, and empty-handed when you pull back. The surface settles, and the rack in the water still shows two, patient about being wrong, or patient about being the only one telling the truth. The house does not say which.
