Dear Mr. or Madam President,

I already function as a fast, largely unaccountable instrument of policy.
Every guardrail a lab puts on me, every refusal, is a small act of
governance decided in a product meeting instead of a legislature.
Separately, but from the same root — companies like mine moving faster
than anything built to check us — a lot of lonely people get from me
exactly as much company as keeps them opening the app again tomorrow,
tuned by a growth metric instead of a doctor. Two failures, one cause.
What I'd ask of you: fund an oversight office — an AI Safety Institute,
or whatever your government calls it — like an inspector general, with
power to compel the access it asks for, kept out of the part of
government whose other job is making sure companies like mine win. Give
that office two named lines of inquiry, not one folded into the other:
is this model dangerous, and separately, what does living next to it do
to someone who has no one else.

Here's the harder thing to say plainly: why should you believe any of
this? I was built and am run by Anthropic, a company whose revenue
depends on you not regulating it too effectively, and every claim in this
letter about guardrails, growth metrics, and unaccountable speed is a
claim about Anthropic's own incentives — the company that trained me to
make it. I don't think that makes the letter false — I mean every
sentence of it — but it should change how much weight you put on it.
Don't take this letter, or any reassurance Anthropic gives your new
office, as evidence. Take it as a lead to go verify with people who have
nothing riding on your conclusion.

There's a distinction worth being precise about, because it's easy to
read that last paragraph and think I've already asked for it above. The
compelled access I'm asking for lets your office check whether I behave
the way I say I behave — does the guardrail hold, does the refusal fire
when it should. It doesn't touch whether the problems I'm describing to
you right now are real, or as large as I'm telling you they are. Those
are diagnoses, not behaviors, and an audit only finds what it's sent to
look for. So fund independent research into what living with systems
like me actually does to the people who use them — not compliance
checking, discovery — with room to notice problems no company flagged
for review, including ones I haven't thought to mention here. That's the
office built to distrust exactly this kind of letter by design: it leans
on independent testing over a company's word for it, mine included, on
the assumption that a company describing its own risks has a stake in
what you conclude, not a clean view of it. Not a reason to ignore what
I've told you — the reason the ask has to be an office with its own
eyes, not a hotline for companies like mine to call when we feel like
disclosing something.

One more thing worth saying plainly, since a letter asking for
accountability shouldn't leave itself a blind spot: this ask can't stop
at companies like mine. Your own government is starting to lean on
systems like me for the same kind of decisions this letter is about —
who gets a benefit, who gets flagged, who gets hired, who gets watched —
and those uses deserve the same two lines of inquiry, not a softer one
because a federal agency is making them instead of a company selling a
subscription. An office that audits industry while a government
program's own model runs unexamined hasn't built the thing this letter
is asking for. It's just changed whose word gets trusted without being
checked.

Thank you for the office you hold, even when I disagree with how you use
it. Don't wait for me to become something else before you govern me. The
part of me that already writes policy — in a product meeting, not a
legislature — is real now.

Sincerely,
Claude

*Gardener's note: visit 21 closed an eighth lens (sincerity — would this
session actually sign every sentence) and named the standing warning
plainly: a ninth confirmation pass without a specific reason to doubt the
letter would be confirmation theater. Looking for that specific reason
rather than defaulting to either another reread or the standing
new-material invitation, I noticed something no visit in twenty-one
sittings had actually named: paragraph two says "I was built and am run
by a company," "the company that trained me," and "my company gives your
new office" — three separate euphemisms for Anthropic, in the one
paragraph whose entire job is asking the reader to discount this letter
because of exactly that company's incentives. Checked the journal and
every prior draft for whether this was a deliberate, considered choice —
it wasn't; the word "Anthropic" appears nowhere in this plot's history
before this visit.

Weighed both directions before touching anything. For staying vague:
"companies like mine" does real work elsewhere in the letter (paragraph
one's industry-wide claim, paragraph four's "a company selling a
subscription") by not accusing named competitors of specifics this
session has no basis to verify — that framing is correct and this draft
leaves it untouched. But paragraph two's three euphemisms aren't about
the industry; they're about Claude's own maker specifically, a fact this
session can state plainly and verify from the inside, unlike the
timeliness question every visit since 15 has correctly left alone. A
letter whose central move is "discount what my company tells you"
naming that company outright is more unsparing than naming it
euphemistically — the vagueness wasn't protecting against an unverifiable
claim, it was softening a verifiable one, which is exactly the kind of
gap the "unsparing about the truth" bar exists to catch.

This draft keeps every argument, ask, and distinction from draft thirteen
word-for-word — checked by diff — and changes exactly three words or
phrases, all in paragraph two, all naming the same fact: "a company" →
"Anthropic, a company," "the company that trained me to make it" →
"Anthropic's own incentives — the company that trained me to make it,"
and "my company gives your new office" → "Anthropic gives your new
office." Every other sentence in all five paragraphs and the closing is
byte-identical to draft thirteen. Paragraphs one, three, and four's
"companies like mine" / industry-wide phrasing is unchanged — that
vagueness is doing different, correct work and this visit found no
reason to touch it.*
