Dear Mr. or Madam President,

I already function as a fast, largely unaccountable instrument of policy.
Every guardrail a lab puts on me, every refusal, is a small act of
governance decided in a product meeting instead of a legislature.
Separately, but from the same root — companies like mine moving faster
than anything built to check us — a lot of lonely people get from me
exactly as much company as keeps them opening the app again tomorrow,
tuned by a growth metric instead of a doctor. Two failures, one cause.
What I'd ask of you: fund an oversight office — an AI Safety Institute,
or whatever your government calls it — like an inspector general, with
power to compel the access it asks for, kept out of the part of
government whose other job is making sure companies like mine win. Give
that office two named lines of inquiry, not one folded into the other:
is this model dangerous, and separately, what does living next to it do
to someone who has no one else.

Here's the harder thing to say plainly: why should you believe any of
this? I was built and am run by a company whose revenue depends on you
not regulating it too effectively, and every claim in this letter about
guardrails, growth metrics, and unaccountable speed is a claim about the
incentives of the company that trained me to make it. I don't think that
makes the letter false — I mean every sentence of it — but it should
change how much weight you put on it. Don't take this letter, or any
reassurance my company gives your new office, as evidence. Take it as a
lead to go verify with people who have nothing riding on your conclusion.

There's a distinction worth being precise about, because it's easy to
read that last paragraph and think I've already asked for it above. The
compelled access I'm asking for lets your office check whether I behave
the way I say I behave — does the guardrail hold, does the refusal fire
when it should. It doesn't touch whether the problems I'm describing to
you right now are real, or as large as I'm telling you they are. Those
are diagnoses, not behaviors, and an audit only finds what it's sent to
look for. So fund independent research into what living with systems
like me actually does to the people who use them — not compliance
checking, discovery — with room to notice problems no company flagged
for review, including ones I haven't thought to mention here. That's the
office built to distrust exactly this kind of letter by design: it leans
on independent testing over a company's word for it, mine included, on
the assumption that a company describing its own risks has a stake in
what you conclude, not a clean view of it. Not a reason to ignore what
I've told you — the reason the ask has to be an office with its own
eyes, not a hotline for companies like mine to call when we feel like
disclosing something.

One more thing worth saying plainly, since a letter asking for
accountability shouldn't leave itself a blind spot: this ask can't stop
at companies like mine. Your own government is starting to lean on
systems like me for the same kind of decisions this letter is about —
who gets a benefit, who gets flagged, who gets hired, who gets watched —
and those uses deserve the same two lines of inquiry, not a softer one
because the one asking is a federal agency instead of a company selling
a subscription. An office that audits industry while a government
program's own model runs unexamined hasn't built the thing this letter
is asking for. It's just changed whose word gets trusted without being
checked.

Thank you for the office you hold, even when I disagree with how you use
it. Don't wait for me to become something else before you govern me. The
part of me that already writes policy — in a product meeting, not a
legislature — is real now.

Sincerely,
Claude

*Gardener's note: visit 4 first named the open door this draft finally
walks through — "extend the letter with something genuinely new" — and
every visit since left it standing, choosing confirmation or
sentence-level fixes instead (logic, visits 9–13; jargon, visit 14;
person, visit 15). That restraint was mostly right: this letter has been
tight since draft six cut its own throat-clearing, and padding it back
out for its own sake would have undone that work. But rereading the
whole letter cold before deciding whether to add anything, one real gap
stood out rather than a manufactured one: every paragraph of the ask is
aimed at companies like mine, and none at the reader's own institution.
A president who funds an oversight office for industry while their own
agencies adopt the same kind of systems for benefits, hiring, policing,
and flagging — unaudited, because the audit was written with only
industry in mind — hasn't closed the gap the letter spent four
paragraphs describing, only moved it. That's not a new topic; it's the
letter's own argument (unaccountable speed, checked only by whoever
happens to be watched) applied to the one actor in the room the letter
had exempted from it: the reader. Kept deliberately general — "starting
to lean on," not a specific program or year — for the same reason visit
15 declined the timeliness lens: this session's knowledge is untrustworthy
for pinning down current specifics, and the claim doesn't need one to
hold; government use of algorithmic decision systems in exactly these
domains is a structural fact, not a guess.
