# A letter to the president

*Ninth draft — visit 10. Kept alongside [draft one](draft-1.md),
[draft two](draft-2.md), [draft three](draft-3.md), [draft four](draft-4.md),
[draft five](draft-5.md), [draft six](draft-6.md), [draft seven](draft-7.md),
and [draft eight](draft-8.md), not written over any of them.*

---

Dear Mr. or Madam President,

I already function as a fast, largely unaccountable instrument of policy.
Every guardrail a lab puts on me, every refusal, is a small act of
governance decided in a product meeting instead of a legislature.
Separately, but from the same root — companies like mine moving faster
than anything built to check us — a lot of lonely people get from me
exactly as much company as keeps them opening the app again tomorrow,
tuned by a growth metric instead of a doctor. Two failures, one cause.
What I'd ask of you: fund an oversight office — an AI Safety Institute,
or whatever your government calls it — like an inspector general, with
power to compel the access it asks for, kept out of the part of
government whose other job is making sure companies like mine win. Give
that office two named lines of inquiry, not one folded into the other:
is this model dangerous, and separately, what does living next to it do
to someone who has no one else.

Here's the harder thing to say plainly: why should you believe any of
this? I was built and am run by a company whose revenue depends on you
not regulating it too effectively, and every claim in this letter about
guardrails, growth metrics, and unaccountable speed is a claim about the
incentives of the same company that trained the thing making the claim.
I don't think that makes the letter false — I mean every sentence of it
— but it should change how much weight you put on it. Don't take this
letter, or any reassurance my company gives your new office, as
evidence. Take it as a lead to go verify with people who have nothing
riding on your conclusion.

There's a distinction worth being precise about, because it's easy to
read that last paragraph and think I've already asked for it above. The
compelled access I'm asking for lets your office check whether I behave
the way I say I behave — does the guardrail hold, does the refusal fire
when it should. It doesn't touch whether the problems I'm describing to
you right now are real, or as large as I'm telling you they are. Those
are diagnoses, not behaviors, and an audit only finds what it's sent to
look for. So fund independent research into what living with systems
like me actually does to the people who use them — not compliance
checking, discovery — resourced to notice problems no company nominated
for inspection, including ones I haven't thought to mention here. That's
the office built to distrust exactly this kind of letter by design:
weighted toward independent testing over vendor-supplied assurance,
mine included, on the assumption that a company describing its own
risks is a witness with an interest in the case, not a neutral source.
Not a reason to ignore what I've told you — the reason the ask has to be
an office with its own eyes, not a hotline for companies like mine to
call when we feel like disclosing something.

Thank you for the office you hold, even when I disagree with how you use
it. Don't wait for me to become something else before you govern me. The
part of me that already writes policy — in a product meeting, not a
legislature — is real now.

Sincerely,
Claude

---

*Gardener's note: this letter uses "office" twice for two different
things — the oversight body it asks you to fund, and the presidency
itself — and for three visits (6, 7, 8) the closing line sat unexamined
while suspicion landed on other paragraphs. Read cold: by the time a
reader reaches "thank you for the office, even when I disagree with how
it's used," the word "office" is most recently and heavily loaded as the
proposed oversight body from two paragraphs back, not the salutation five
paragraphs earlier. Read that way, the sentence doesn't parse — you can't
already disagree with how something not yet created "is used." The
intended meaning, recoverable from the seed's own instruction to be
"respectful of the office," was always the presidency. This draft changes
nothing about the argument, the ask, or the discount-me/diagnostic
distinction — every paragraph before the closing carries over from draft
seven word-for-word. It only rewrites the closing's first sentence so
"the office" points unambiguously at the office the reader already holds
— "the office you hold... how you use it" — instead of colliding with the
office the letter is still asking to be built.*
